6. CONCLUSION

This thesis set out to answer a question that sounds deceptively simple. If you change the lane markings in a driving video, does the car drive differently? Answering it required building something that did not previously exist, a complete pipeline that segments road surfaces, generates lane markings from text descriptions, and analyses the downstream behavioural response through a trajectory prediction model, and then running it at a scale large enough to draw meaningful conclusions. This chapter summarises what was built, what was found, what it means beyond this specific study, and what questions remain open.


6.1. Summary of Contributions

The work produced six concrete contributions, each corresponding to a research goal defined in Chapter 1.

First, an automated road segmentation pipeline based on SAM 2's (cf. Ravi et al., 2024) video predictor with a geometric seed-point strategy that requires no manual annotation, no per-video prompts, and no external detection models. The six-point bottom-half grid, anchored at the bottom centre of the frame, exploits the structural regularity of dashcam footage and achieved a 100 percent success rate across 100 diverse driving videos, with a median processing time of 2.73 seconds per video. This contribution answers RQ1 and fulfils Goal 1.

Second, a dual-model video inpainting architecture that separates first-frame semantic precision from temporal propagation. FluxFill generates a single frame with the correct lane marking attributes, and CogVideoX propagates that frame through time via VideoPainter's (cf. Bian et al., 2025) context encoding mechanism. This design emerged from the practical observation that neither model alone satisfies both requirements. CogVideoX (cf. Yang et al., 2024) produces temporally coherent but semantically imprecise output, while FluxFill (cf. Esser et al., 2024) produces semantically precise but temporally isolated frames. The combination achieved temporal consistency above 0.985 and CLIP Scores (cf. Radford et al., 2021) of 19 to 23 across 1,000 edited videos. This addresses RQ2 and fulfils Goal 2.

Third, a VLM-based iterative quality assurance loop where Qwen 2.5-VL evaluates each generated first frame against the editing instruction and refines the caption when the result is unsatisfactory. A key technical detail is relevant here. VideoPainter's original evaluation code uses OpenAI's GPT-4 through a proprietary API, which creates external dependencies and data governance concerns. This thesis replaced all such API calls with the locally running Qwen 2.5-VL-7B (cf. Bai et al., 2025), making the quality assurance loop fully self-contained, with no API keys, no per-call costs, no data leaving the pipeline's own infrastructure. For solid-line prompts, this mechanism achieved pass rates of 89 to 100 percent. For the single dashed prompt, it reached only 49 percent, exposing a fundamental limitation in the base generative model's ability to produce geometrically complex patterns, which no amount of prompt refinement can overcome. This answers RQ3 and fulfils Goal 3.

Fourth, an automated data generation and filtering pipeline that uses SAM 2 and Qwen 2.5-VL to create curated training datasets for domain-specific LoRA (cf. Hu et al., 2022) fine-tuning of FluxFill. Five LoRA adapters, one per lane marking type, were trained in parallel with the main experiments, with training losses converging within three to five epochs. This fulfils Goal 4.

Fifth, a VLA behavioural responsiveness analysis that uses Alpamayo (cf. NVIDIA et al., 2025), a 10-billion-parameter VLA trajectory prediction model, to verify that the generated lane markings are perceived by a downstream driving model. The dual-pass protocol, original versus edited frames, with all non-target cameras blacked out, confirmed that the VLA model does not ignore the synthetic content. Per-video trajectory shifts on the order of one metre were observed across all experiments. However, because the generated lanes are intentionally different from the originals, the trajectory delta against original ground truth does not measure generation quality. CLIP Score and temporal consistency serve as the meaningful quality metrics. This addresses RQ5 and fulfils Goal 5.

Sixth, a containerised, cloud-native orchestration framework that ran the three-stage pipeline end-to-end on Mercedes-Benz Helix infrastructure, with shared run identifiers providing full data lineage from source video to trajectory prediction. The pipeline processed 1,000 edited videos and 2,000 trajectory inference passes without a single pipeline-level failure. This answers RQ6 and fulfils Goals 6 and 7.


6.2. Impact on Industry

The most immediate industrial application is in ADAS training data diversification. Automotive OEMs and suppliers maintain large fleets that record driving data continuously, but the lane marking configurations in those recordings reflect the geography where the fleet operates. A European fleet will produce data dominated by white markings on motorways; North American markings, Asian road standards, or degraded rural lines will be underrepresented. The pipeline demonstrated in this thesis offers a way to augment these datasets by editing the lane markings in existing footage, producing photorealistic videos with controlled attributes that the models need but the fleet has not encountered.

The practical value hinges on a finding from Chapter 5. The VLA trajectory prediction model does not ignore the synthetic lane markings. Per-video trajectory shifts of roughly one metre confirm that the generated content is visually significant enough to alter driving behaviour predictions, which means the generated data is perceptually consequential, not merely cosmetically different. Whether the altered trajectories are appropriate for the generated lane configurations requires visual inspection on a per-video basis, but the mere fact that the VLA responds confirms functional relevance. For an industry where the cost of physical data collection runs into millions of euros per campaign, the ability to generate controlled variations from existing footage at a marginal compute cost of roughly 130 GPU-hours per 1,000 videos represents a meaningful efficiency gain.

The containerised architecture is designed for integration into existing industrial data pipelines. Each stage exposes a well-defined interface (video in, masks out; masks and prompt in, edited video out; video in, trajectory out), and the orchestration layer can be adapted to any workflow engine. The pipeline ran on standard NVIDIA A100 GPUs within a production cloud environment, requiring no specialised hardware beyond what most automotive AI teams already have access to. This lowers the barrier to adoption considerably compared with approaches that require custom simulation engines or proprietary rendering infrastructure.

A secondary industrial application is in validation and edge-case testing. By generating videos with specific lane marking configurations and measuring the trajectory response, development teams can systematically probe how their models react to particular road features, including rare or novel marking types, without waiting for those features to appear in recorded data. This is a form of targeted scenario generation that complements existing simulation- based testing approaches by operating on real-world visual data rather than rendered scenes.


6.3. Impact on Society and the Scientific Community

The societal relevance of this work lies in its potential contribution to road safety. Lane keeping and trajectory planning are among the most safety-critical functions in modern vehicles. If the models governing these functions have been trained on data that lacks diversity in lane marking configurations, their behaviour in underrepresented scenarios is uncertain, and uncertain behaviour in a two-tonne vehicle moving at highway speeds is a safety concern. By providing a mechanism to diversify the training data that these models consume, the pipeline addresses one pathway through which data imbalance translates into real-world risk.

For the scientific community, three methodological contributions are worth highlighting.

The first is the use of a VLA trajectory model as a behavioural probe for video inpainting in autonomous driving. The conventional evaluation suite, PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS, measures pixel-level fidelity, while CLIP Score and temporal consistency measure semantic alignment and temporal coherence respectively; these are the meaningful metrics for generation quality. The trajectory analysis adds a different dimension. It confirms that a downstream driving model perceives and responds to the generated lane markings. The finding that pixel-level metrics are nearly invariant across lane marking types (less than 0.03 dB PSNR variation) while trajectory predictions shift by one metre per video demonstrates that visual significance and pixel-level similarity are decoupled. This dual-pass protocol can be adapted to other editing targets and downstream tasks as a behavioural responsiveness check, verifying that generated content is not invisible to the consuming model, though it does not by itself measure the quality of the generated data.

The second is the VLM-in-the-loop refinement pattern. Using a vision-language model as an automated quality critic within the generation pipeline, rather than as a post-hoc evaluator, is a design pattern that has broader applicability. Any text-guided generation task where the output must satisfy a verifiable specification (not just lane markings, but road signs, building facades, medical imaging features) could benefit from a similar loop. The finding that this pattern works well for simple visual attributes but fails for geometrically complex ones provides a useful boundary condition for future implementations.

The third is the empirical demonstration that independently developed foundation models, SAM 2, CogVideoX, Alpamayo, can be composed into a functioning pipeline without joint training or architectural modification. This supports the broader thesis in the AI community that foundation models are converging toward interoperable building blocks, and that practical systems can be assembled by chaining them through well-defined interfaces rather than by training monolithic end-to-end models.


6.4. Strengths and Weaknesses in Retrospect

The methodological strengths identified in Chapter 3, reproducibility, automation, behavioural responsiveness analysis and modularity, did manifest in the findings largely as expected. Reproducibility was confirmed by the consistency between the 100frames and vps90 variants. The two processing modes produced nearly identical quality metrics across all five prompts, with differences well below any threshold of practical significance. Automation was confirmed by the zero-failure segmentation rate and the ability to process 1,000 videos without human intervention at any stage. The trajectory analysis confirmed that the VLA model perceives and responds to the generated lane markings, a behavioural observation that pixel-level metrics alone would not have revealed. Modularity was confirmed by the fact that the segmentation stage, the inpainting stage and the evaluation stage were developed, debugged and updated independently throughout the project.

The methodological weaknesses also manifested, and in some cases more sharply than anticipated. The full-road mask strategy was expected to make the inpainting task harder, and it did: PSNR and SSIM values confirm that replacing the entire road surface produces large pixel-level deviations even when the lane markings themselves are correct. What was less expected was how decisively the dashed-line prompt (p5) would expose the limits of both the generative model and the VLM evaluator. The roughly 50 percent failure rate for p5 was not a subtle signal; it was a clear boundary between what base FluxFill can generate reliably and what it cannot. This was the most practically useful unexpected finding of the study, because it identifies exactly where domain-specific fine-tuning is most needed and provides a concrete, measurable criterion for assessing whether LoRA adaptation has succeeded.

A noteworthy observation was the near-symmetry of trajectory shifts. The initial expectation was that replacing real-world lane markings with synthetic ones would systematically shift trajectories away from the original ground truth. Instead, approximately 45 percent of videos showed trajectories closer to the original ground truth after editing, while 55 percent diverged further. However, this symmetry should be interpreted cautiously. Because the generated lanes are intentionally different from the originals, a trajectory that diverges from the original ground truth is not necessarily "worse". It may simply reflect the VLA model correctly responding to different lane geometry. The per-video trajectory shifts of roughly one metre in either direction confirm that the generated lane markings are visually significant to the driving model, but assessing whether any individual trajectory is appropriate for its corresponding generated lane configuration requires visual inspection.

The most consequential unexpected result concerned LoRA fine-tuning. The hypothesis that domain-specific adaptation would improve lane marking generation quality was tested through a complete training-and-inference cycle (Chapter 4, Section 4.11). The outcome was unambiguously negative: LoRA-adapted FluxFill produced worse perceptual metrics (SSIM 0.487 vs. 0.510, LPIPS 0.487 vs. 0.458) and lower CLIP Scores (21.25 vs. 21.72), with the trajectory analysis showing a 3.8-fold larger mean ADE shift (+0.61 m vs. +0.16 m). The most probable cause, discussed in Chapter 5, Section 5.10, is contamination of the automated training data by confident misclassifications from the Qwen 2.5-VL captioning pipeline. This finding carries a practical lesson that extends beyond the specific pipeline: automated training data generation via VLM captioning requires rigorous quality validation before use in fine-tuning, and evaluation must extend beyond single-frame quality metrics to capture downstream task performance.


6.5. Directions for Future Research

The findings and limitations of this study suggest several concrete directions for further work.

LoRA training data quality and re-training. The LoRA inference experiments (Chapter 4, Section 4.11; discussed in Chapter 5, Section 5.10) produced a counterproductive outcome: the domain-adapted FluxFill models performed worse than the base model on every quality metric (CLIP Score, SSIM, LPIPS), with the trajectory analysis also showing a 3.8-fold larger mean ADE shift. The most probable cause identified in Chapter 5 is false positives in the automated training data: the Qwen 2.5-VL captioning pipeline produced confident misclassifications that contaminated the LoRA training sets. The immediate next step is a manual audit of the training datasets to quantify the false positive rate, followed by re-training with verified-correct samples only. If cleaned training data resolves the quality gap, this would confirm that the LoRA approach itself is sound and that the negative result reflects a data curation failure rather than a fundamental limitation. If the gap persists, higher-rank adaptation (rank 32 or 64) or full fine-tuning should be explored to determine whether the rank-16 bottleneck is the binding constraint.

Lane-specific masking. Replacing the full-road mask with a lane-specific mask, where only the lane marking pixels are targeted for editing, would fundamentally change the inpainting task. The model would need to generate only the markings themselves rather than the entire road surface, which should be a substantially easier problem and would yield more interpretable pixel-level metrics. Implementing this requires a lane detection model (CLRerNet or a similar architecture) upstream of the inpainting stage, which introduces additional complexity but would provide a much more precise assessment of lane marking generation quality.

Multi-camera evaluation. The single-camera black-out protocol used in this thesis isolates the effect of the edits but does not reflect how a production system would respond. Running Alpamayo with all seven cameras active, the front telephoto showing edited frames while the other six show real footage, would measure the diluted but ecologically valid impact of the edits. This experiment would establish whether lane marking changes on a single camera view produce measurable trajectory effects even when the model has access to redundant visual information from other angles.

Expanded lane marking vocabulary. The five prompts used here cover the basic attribute combinations of count, colour and pattern, but real-world lane markings include many additional configurations: dotted lines, chevrons, turn arrows, bike lane symbols, construction-zone markings, and degraded or partially occluded lines. Extending the prompt vocabulary and testing the pipeline's ability to generate these more complex configurations would characterise the scope of what text-guided video inpainting can achieve for driving data augmentation.

Cross-dataset generalisation. Running the pipeline on footage from different cameras, geographic regions, weather conditions and road types would test whether the findings reported here, the segmentation success rate, the attribute-quality hierarchy, the trajectory impact patterns, hold beyond the PhysicalAI-AV dataset. Of particular interest would be footage recorded under adverse conditions (rain, snow, night) where lane marking visibility is reduced and the effect of adding synthetic markings on trajectory prediction might be amplified.

Downstream training impact. This thesis measures the effect of edited videos on trajectory prediction at inference time. The natural follow-up is to measure the effect on training: does including the generated lane marking variations in the training set of a lane detection model (e.g., CLRerNet) or a trajectory prediction model improve performance on underrepresented marking types? This would close the full augmentation loop, from generation to training to evaluation, and provide the strongest possible evidence for the practical utility of the approach.

Explicit geometric constraints for video inpainting. The temporal consistency of generated videos currently relies entirely on CogVideoX's learned temporal modelling. Introducing explicit constraints, such as enforcing that lane marking positions remain fixed in road coordinates across frames, or that dashed patterns repeat at metrically consistent intervals, could improve the geometric fidelity of the generated content. This would require either conditioning the video diffusion model on road geometry information or applying post-processing that corrects geometric inconsistencies while preserving visual quality.

Real-time integration. All experiments in this thesis were conducted offline. Integrating the pipeline, or a distilled version of it, into a real-time ADAS testing loop, where edited videos are generated on-the-fly and fed to a running driving system, would open a path to continuous, automated testing of perception robustness against lane marking variations.


6.6. Take-Home Messages

This thesis has demonstrated, across 1,000 edited videos and 2,000 trajectory evaluation passes, that text-guided video inpainting can produce lane marking variations in real driving footage and that those variations measurably change how a state-of-the-art trajectory prediction model behaves. Three findings stand out as the clearest messages from this work.

First, synthetic lane markings are not invisible to downstream driving models. The trajectory prediction changes, per-video deltas on the order of one metre, with 40 to 52 percent of videos showing improvement, establish that generative video editing produces content that is functionally consequential for autonomous driving, not merely visually plausible. For an industry investing heavily in data-driven perception, this means that generative augmentation of lane markings is a viable path to training data diversification, with effects that propagate beyond appearance into driving behaviour.

Second, visual quality metrics and functional impact metrics tell different stories. PSNR, SSIM and LPIPS were nearly identical across all five lane marking types; CLIP Score revealed a four-point spread; and trajectory prediction showed per-video effects that varied by a full metre. For any safety-critical application of generative models, evaluating only how the output looks, without measuring what it does, leaves the most important question unanswered.

Third, the pipeline works end-to-end without human intervention, which is ultimately what determines whether a research contribution can become an engineering tool. One hundred videos were segmented, edited with five different lane marking prompts, and evaluated for trajectory impact, all within a containerised cloud environment with full data lineage, and not a single step required manual input. The gap between research prototype and industrial utility, while not fully closed, is narrow enough that the next steps are engineering decisions rather than scientific unknowns.
