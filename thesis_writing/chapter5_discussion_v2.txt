5. DISCUSSION OF FINDINGS

The three-stage pipeline produced 1,000 edited videos from 100 source clips, achieved a 100 percent segmentation success rate with no manual intervention, and demonstrated that the Alpamayo VLA model responds to the generated lane markings across all five prompts. Inpainting quality, measured by the meaningful evaluation metrics of CLIP Score and temporal consistency, was consistent at the pixel level but diverged meaningfully at the semantic level, with CLIP Score separating prompt types by up to four points and VLM caption refinement exposing a clear boundary between solid and dashed lane patterns. Trajectory analysis confirmed that the VLA model perceives the synthetic lane markings, with per-video trajectory shifts of roughly one metre, though the direction and magnitude of these shifts varied at the individual video level.

This chapter interprets these findings in the context of the research questions posed in Chapter 1, examines the methodological limitations that bound the conclusions, compares the results with the expectations set by the literature reviewed in Chapter 2, and discusses the extent to which the findings transfer beyond the specific experimental conditions of this study.


5.1. Research Question 1: Automated Road Segmentation

RQ1 asked whether an automated video segmentation pipeline could produce road surface masks of sufficient quality for downstream video inpainting without manual annotation or interactive prompts.

The answer, based on the Run 006 results, is unambiguously yes. All 100 videos were segmented successfully by SAM 2.1 Hiera-Large (cf. Ravi et al., 2024) using the six-point bottom-half grid, with no failures and no human involvement at any stage. The median processing time of 2.73 seconds per video means that the segmentation stage adds negligible overhead to the overall pipeline. It accounts for less than one percent of the total per-video processing time when compared with the VideoPainter stage, which takes roughly 7.5 minutes per video for solid-line prompts.

The design choice that made this possible was the geometric prior: placing seed points exclusively in the bottom half of the frame, anchored at the bottom centre, exploits the fact that in any front-facing dashcam view the road surface always occupies the lower portion of the image. This eliminates the need for an external detection model (the Grounding DINO approach that was attempted and discarded, as discussed in Chapter 2) and makes the segmentation prompt-free in the sense that no per-video input is required. SAM 2's streaming memory architecture then propagates this initial mask through the entire video with temporal consistency, producing the smooth, coherent binary masks that the inpainting stage requires.

Two outlier videos consumed disproportionate processing time; 168 and 599 seconds respectively, compared with the 2.73-second median, but still produced valid outputs. These outliers account for 74 percent of the total segmentation wall time, which suggests that under certain conditions (potentially unusual road geometry or lighting) the model requires more iterations to converge on a stable mask. Investigating the causes of these outliers was beyond the scope of Run 006, but their existence does not undermine the overall conclusion. The pipeline handles them gracefully without failure, which is the property that matters for automated operation at scale.

The practical implication is that SAM 2's video predictor, when combined with a well-chosen geometric prompt strategy, can serve as a reliable first stage in video editing pipelines for autonomous driving data. The zero-failure rate across 100 diverse videos provides reasonable confidence that this approach scales beyond the current sample, though the caveat about dataset scope discussed in Section 5.5 applies.


5.2. Research Question 2: Dual-Model Inpainting Architecture

RQ2 asked whether a dual-model inpainting architecture, with separate models for first-frame generation and temporal propagation, produces semantically more accurate lane markings than a single video diffusion model alone.

This question cannot be answered with a formal ablation from the Run 006 data, because the production experiments did not include a CogVideoX-only baseline (cf. Yang et al., 2024) (i.e., text-conditioned inpainting without the FluxFill (cf. Esser et al., 2024) first frame). The answer therefore rests on the development-phase observations documented in Chapter 3, supplemented by the indirect evidence in the Run 006 metrics.

During development, the rationale for the dual-model approach emerged from two complementary failure modes. When CogVideoX was used alone for text-guided video inpainting, it produced temporally smooth outputs, the generated content did not flicker or shift between frames, but the lane markings in the first frame were often semantically imprecise. Lines might appear with the wrong count, colour or pattern, and once established in the first frame, these errors propagated through the entire video because CogVideoX's temporal coherence mechanism faithfully preserved whatever it generated initially. Conversely, FluxFill alone produced semantically precise first frames, the lane markings matched the text prompt in count, colour and pattern, but it operates on single images and has no mechanism for temporal propagation. Processing each frame independently with FluxFill would have required a separate denoising pass per frame and offered no guarantee of temporal consistency between frames.

The dual-model architecture resolves this by assigning each model to its strength. FluxFill generates the semantically correct first frame, and CogVideoX propagates that frame through time via VideoPainter's context encoding mechanism. The Run 006 results are consistent with this design hypothesis. Temporal consistency exceeds 0.985 in every experiment, confirming that CogVideoX maintains visual coherence across frames. CLIP Scores (cf. Radford et al., 2021) of 19 to 23 indicate that the generated content has measurable text-video alignment, though the degree of alignment varies with prompt complexity (discussed under RQ4). The VLM caption refinement pass rates of 89 to 100 percent for solid-line prompts (Table 4.3) suggest that the first frame is semantically correct in the large majority of cases, which would not be expected if the first-frame generation were poor.

The limitation here is one of experimental design rather than outcome. A rigorous answer to RQ2 would require a controlled comparison against the same 100 videos processed with CogVideoX-only inpainting under the same prompts and evaluation protocol. Such a comparison was not conducted because the development-phase observations made the single-model approach clearly unviable for the lane marking use case, and the available compute budget was allocated to the full factorial experiment instead. Future work could address this gap with a dedicated ablation study.


5.3. Research Question 3: VLM-Based Quality Assessment

RQ3 asked whether a vision-language model can serve as an effective automated quality assessor within an iterative prompt refinement loop, and whether this mechanism improves the semantic correctness of the generated output.

The caption refinement data from Table 4.3 provide a nuanced answer. Yes, but with a sharp performance boundary that depends on lane marking pattern complexity.

Before discussing the results, it is worth reiterating a technical point from Chapter 3. The VLM used in the refinement loop is Qwen 2.5-VL-7B (cf. Bai et al., 2025), running locally within the pipeline's container. VideoPainter's original codebase relies on OpenAI's GPT-4 API for visual evaluation, which would have introduced an external dependency, per-call costs, and data governance concerns. Replacing those API calls with a self-contained open-source VLM running on the same GPU is itself a practical contribution. It makes the entire quality assurance mechanism reproducible, cost-free at inference time, and deployable in restricted industrial environments. The results below therefore reflect what a fully local, open-source VLM can achieve in this role, without any reliance on proprietary services.

For solid-line prompts (p1 through p4), the Qwen 2.5-VL evaluator achieves pass rates between 89 and 100 percent across both processing variants. This means that in the vast majority of cases, the iterative loop either confirms the initial FluxFill output as correct or successfully refines the caption until the regenerated output satisfies the VLM's semantic criteria. For these relatively straightforward visual patterns, continuous lines of a specified colour and count, the VLM-based quality gate works as intended.

Prompt p5, the single dashed white line, tells a different story. Only 49 out of 100 videos received a PASS verdict in the 100frames variant, and 46 out of 100 in the vps90 variant. Roughly half the videos exhausted the maximum 10 refinement iterations without producing an output that the VLM judged acceptable. There are two possible explanations, which are not mutually exclusive.

The first explanation concerns the generative model's capability. Dashed lane markings are geometrically more complex than solid lines. They require a repeating pattern of evenly spaced segments rather than a single continuous stroke. FluxFill, as a general-purpose image inpainting model without domain-specific training, may simply struggle to produce this pattern consistently. If the base model cannot generate geometrically regular dashed lines, no amount of caption refinement will fix the problem, because the issue is in the generation rather than the specification.

The second explanation concerns the VLM evaluator itself. The VLM must distinguish between a solid line and a dashed line in a generated road scene, which requires fine-grained spatial reasoning about line continuity. It is possible that the VLM's assessment is overly strict for dashed patterns, rejecting outputs that a human observer would consider acceptable, or that its feedback loops fail to converge because the refinement suggestions do not address the underlying generative limitation.

The timing data from Table 4.4 support the first explanation. Prompt p5 generates a median processing time of 857 to 879 seconds, roughly double the median for solid-line prompts (432 to 465 seconds). This excess time is entirely attributable to repeated caption refinement iterations and re-generation attempts, confirming that the loop is actively working (not silently failing) but is unable to produce satisfactory results in many cases. The pipeline is spending twice as long trying to get dashed lines right and still failing half the time.

The practical conclusion is that VLM-based iterative refinement is an effective quality mechanism for visual patterns that the underlying generative model can produce reliably, but it cannot compensate for fundamental limitations in the generative model's capability. The LoRA (cf. Hu et al., 2022) inference results (Section 4.11) now provide a direct test of the hypothesis that domain-specific fine-tuning would improve p5 performance. The result is negative, the LoRA-adapted FluxFill achieves a pass rate of only 6 out of 20 (30%) for p5, compared with 49 out of 100 (49%) for the base model, worse, not better. This definitively rules out the hypothesis that the base model's general-purpose nature was the primary cause of the dashed-pattern failure. The dashed-line generation problem appears to be a more fundamental architectural limitation of FluxFill's diffusion process, which domain-specific LoRA training at rank 16 with the available training data (373 dashed-line images) cannot overcome. The VLM refinement loop correctly identifies the persistent generation failures but cannot resolve them through caption adjustment alone.


5.4. Research Question 4: Lane Marking Attributes and Inpainting Quality

RQ4 asked how different lane marking attributes, count, colour and pattern, affect inpainting quality as measured by text-video alignment, temporal consistency and VLM-based assessment.

The answer depends on which metric is examined. Pixel-level and structural metrics (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS) are almost entirely insensitive to lane marking attributes. The inter-prompt variation in PSNR is less than 0.03 dB, in SSIM less than 0.001, and in LPIPS less than 0.003. These metrics measure global image similarity rather than the semantic content of the generated lane markings, so they respond primarily to the fact that the same road region is being replaced (which produces similar levels of overall change regardless of what replaces it) rather than to the specifics of the replacement content. Temporal consistency is similarly uniform, exceeding 0.985 for every experiment. CogVideoX's temporal modelling maintains visual coherence regardless of the lane content being propagated.

CLIP Score, by contrast, reveals a clear hierarchy. Prompt p1 (single solid white) achieves the highest alignment at 23.21, followed by p5 (single dashed white) at 22.73, p2 (double solid white) at 21.89, p3 (single yellow solid) at 21.57, and p4 (double solid yellow) at the bottom with 19.21. This ranking is perfectly consistent between the 100frames and vps90 variants. Three patterns emerge from this ordering.

First, colour matters. White-line prompts (p1, p2, p5) consistently outscore yellow-line prompts (p3, p4). White lane markings are far more prevalent in the training data of the underlying models (both FluxFill and the CLIP model used for scoring), so higher alignment for white configurations is expected. The gap is roughly two CLIP points for matched configurations (p1 vs. p3, single solid; p2 vs. p4, double solid), which is consistent across experiments.

Second, count matters. Single-line prompts outscore double-line prompts within each colour group. P1 exceeds p2 by 1.3 points, and p3 exceeds p4 by 2.4 points. Generating two parallel lines with correct spacing is a harder visual task than generating one, and the CLIP embedding captures this difference.

Third, pattern complexity has a compound effect that manifests most clearly in the VLM assessment. Prompt p5 achieves a reasonable CLIP Score (22.73) but fails the VLM evaluation half the time. The CLIP metric evaluates holistic text-image similarity in an embedding space that may not be sensitive to the distinction between solid and dashed patterns, both are "white lines on a road", while the VLM evaluation requires explicit visual verification of the repeating pattern. This divergence between CLIP Score and VLM assessment highlights a practical limitation of CLIP as a standalone quality metric for fine-grained visual attributes. It captures semantic category but not geometric detail.

The combined picture suggests a hierarchy of difficulty for text-guided lane marking generation. Single solid white is easiest, followed by other solid configurations with increasing difficulty as colour and count deviate from the most common pattern, and dashed patterns are hardest regardless of other attributes. This hierarchy is useful for practitioners planning to use similar pipelines. Solid-line generation with base models is likely to produce acceptable results, while dashed or more complex geometric patterns will benefit from domain-specific fine-tuning.


5.5. Research Question 5: VLA Responsiveness to Lane Marking Edits

RQ5 asked what the quantitative impact of text-guided lane marking modifications is on trajectory prediction, and whether this impact varies across lane marking types.

An important caveat is necessary before interpreting the trajectory data. The minADE metric compares predicted trajectories against the ground-truth trajectory recorded with the original lane markings. Because the generated lane markings are intentionally different from the originals, the "correct" trajectory for the edited scene may itself differ from the original ground truth. A trajectory change after editing therefore does not indicate that the generated video is better or worse: it indicates that the VLA model perceives and responds to the modified visual content. Whether the predicted trajectory is appropriate for the generated lane configuration can only be assessed through visual inspection, not through the minADE delta. Consequently, trajectory analysis serves as a behavioural responsiveness check, confirming that the VLA model treats the generated lane markings as meaningful visual input, rather than as a quality metric for the generated data. The meaningful quantitative metrics for generation quality are CLIP Score and temporal consistency.

With this framing, the trajectory results from Chapter 4 can be interpreted as follows. The baseline minADE across the 100 original videos was 3.778 metres. After editing, the mean minADE shifted by +0.063 to +0.286 metres for the 100frames variant, with an overall average delta of +0.16 metres (Table 4.9). The existence of a measurable delta, regardless of its sign, confirms that the Alpamayo VLA model does not ignore the synthetic lane markings; it incorporates them into its trajectory prediction.

The delta decomposition in Table 4.8 provides further detail. In every experiment, approximately 40 to 52 percent of videos show a negative delta (the model predicts trajectories closer to the original ground truth from the edited frames) while the remainder show a positive delta (trajectories diverge further from the original ground truth). The per-video deltas, on the order of one metre in either direction, are substantially larger than the aggregate mean delta, which hovers near zero because positive and negative shifts partially cancel out.

This pattern is consistent with the VLA model actively interpreting the generated lane markings rather than ignoring them. The synthetic lanes provide different visual cues than the originals, so different trajectory predictions are expected. Whether those changed trajectories are appropriate for the generated lane configuration is a question that cannot be answered by comparing against the original ground truth: it requires visual inspection of each video to assess whether the predicted path aligns with the lane geometry actually rendered.

Across prompts in the 100frames variant, the magnitude of trajectory change varies: p1 shows the smallest mean delta (+0.063 m) and p5 the largest (+0.286 m). Prompt p5's larger trajectory shift is consistent with its lower CLIP Score and caption pass rate. When the generated lane markings deviate more from the text prompt, the VLA model's trajectory response is correspondingly larger.

The vps90 variant shows uniformly higher mean deltas (+0.24 on average compared with +0.16 for 100frames). This is consistent with the fact that the vps90 variant edits more frames: a longer window of modified visual input produces a larger behavioural shift in the VLA model.

The key insight from RQ5 is that the Alpamayo VLA model does not ignore synthetic lane markings: the null hypothesis that the generated content would be invisible to downstream trajectory prediction is rejected by the data. The per-video trajectory shifts of roughly one metre confirm that the generated lane markings are visually significant enough to alter driving behaviour predictions. However, whether these trajectory changes represent correct driving behaviour for the generated lane configuration cannot be determined from the minADE metric alone and would require per-video visual inspection. For evaluating the quality of the generated video data itself, CLIP Score and temporal consistency remain the appropriate quantitative metrics.


5.6. Research Question 6: Pipeline Orchestration Feasibility

RQ6 asked whether it is feasible to orchestrate a three-stage pipeline across heterogeneous containerised environments into a reproducible, cloud-native workflow suitable for industrial ADAS development.

The Run 006 execution provides a practical demonstration that it is. The pipeline ran end-to-end across three containerised stages, namely SAM 2, VideoPainter (cf. Bian et al., 2025) and Alpamayo (cf. NVIDIA et al., 2025), each with its own Docker image, dependency environment and GPU requirements. The shared run_id linked every output artefact to its source video and processing parameters, providing full data lineage from input video to final trajectory prediction. All configuration, point grids, prompts, hyperparameters, camera settings, was specified declaratively and versioned, making the experiment reproducible.

The orchestration was managed through the Helix workflow engine running on the Mercedes-Benz cloud infrastructure, with Google Cloud Storage providing the shared data layer between stages. Each stage was independently deployable and could be updated without affecting the others, which was practically useful during development when the inpainting stage underwent several iterations while the segmentation stage remained stable.

Two quantitative observations speak to the engineering maturity of the orchestration. First, the pipeline processed 1,000 videos and 2,000 trajectory inference passes without a single pipeline-level failure. Individual stages occasionally required retries (the SAM 2 outliers being the most visible example), but the orchestration layer handled these transparently. Second, the total compute footprint for the full experiment, roughly 130 GPU-hours for inpainting, 17 minutes for segmentation, and 50 minutes for trajectory evaluation per variant, is manageable on a modest GPU allocation, making the pipeline viable for regular use in an industrial setting rather than being limited to one-off research experiments.

The limitation is that "feasibility" at the scale of 100 videos does not automatically guarantee performance at the scale of thousands or tens of thousands. The sequential per-GPU processing model used in Run 006 would require parallelisation across multiple GPUs for significantly larger datasets, and the GCS-based data transfer model may become a bottleneck if upload and download times grow with dataset size. These scaling considerations are engineering challenges rather than fundamental obstacles, but they have not been empirically validated.


5.7. Comparison with Literature

The findings from Run 006, while internally consistent, sit in a different context from the benchmark results reported in the publications reviewed in Chapter 2. Understanding where the results align with prior work and where they diverge is important for interpreting what the pipeline actually achieves.

The most direct comparison is with VideoPainter's published performance on the DAVIS benchmark (Bian et al., 2025), with PSNR 33.75 dB, SSIM 0.982, LPIPS 0.016 and CLIP Score 28.79 for text-guided editing. The corresponding values from Run 006, namely PSNR around 11 dB, SSIM around 0.509, LPIPS around 0.458, and CLIP Scores of 19 to 23, are dramatically lower across every metric. However, this comparison is misleading if taken at face value. DAVIS evaluations use small object-level masks (a person, a car, a bicycle) that cover a fraction of the frame, and the metrics compare the edited output against a known ground truth where the object was merely removed or replaced with something similar. In this thesis, the mask covers the entire visible road surface, often 40 to 60 percent of the frame area, and the generated content is intentionally different from what was there before. The pipeline is not reconstructing; it is creating new semantic content according to a text specification. PSNR and SSIM, which measure pixel-level agreement with the original, are expected to be low in this setting because the goal is change, not preservation. The relevant takeaway is not that the pipeline performs poorly relative to DAVIS benchmarks, but that pixel-level metrics are structurally unsuited for evaluating semantic editing tasks and should not be treated as indicators of generation quality in this context.

CLIP Score provides a fairer comparison because it measures semantic alignment rather than pixel fidelity. The Run 006 scores of 19 to 23 fall below VideoPainter's 28.79 on DAVIS, but the gap is narrower, and the tasks are sufficiently different that a direct numeric comparison is of limited value. Lane marking descriptions ("single solid white continuous line") are highly specific spatial instructions, while DAVIS text prompts tend to describe general object appearance. Whether the CLIP embedding space is equally discriminative for both types of description is an open question. What can be said is that CLIP Score successfully differentiates between lane marking prompts in the Run 006 data, the four-point spread from p1 to p4 is consistent and reproducible, which confirms its utility as a relative quality metric within this domain, even if absolute values are not directly comparable with published benchmarks.

Temporal consistency in the Run 006 results (>0.985) confirms the expectation set by CogVideoX's architecture. The Expert Transformer (Yang et al., 2024) was designed with 3D full attention precisely to maintain temporal coherence across generated video frames. The near-unity temporal consistency scores validate this design claim: regardless of what content is being generated, the temporal propagation mechanism preserves visual continuity. This is a strong result, particularly given that the masks in this thesis cover a much larger proportion of the frame than typical video inpainting benchmarks, which means CogVideoX must maintain coherence over a larger region of generated content.

The use of Alpamayo as a behavioural responsiveness probe has no direct precedent in the reviewed literature. Alpamayo was evaluated (NVIDIA et al., 2025) on the nuScenes benchmark and demonstrated competitive minADE performance, but the model was always used in its intended role: predicting trajectories from real-world driving data. The dual-pass protocol used here, where the model processes the same ego-motion history with different visual inputs and the minADE delta serves as the responsiveness signal, is a novel application. However, it is important to note that comparing trajectories against the original ground truth does not measure generation quality: the generated lanes are intentionally different and should produce different trajectories. The value of the trajectory analysis lies in confirming that the VLA model treats the generated content as meaningful visual input: the per-video deltas are on the order of one metre, demonstrating that the synthetic lane markings are not ignored by the model. Whether the resulting trajectories are appropriate for the specific generated lane configurations can only be assessed through visual inspection. CLIP Score and temporal consistency remain the quantitative metrics for evaluating the generated video data itself.

The LoRA training results are consistent with the expectations from the original LoRA paper (Hu et al., 2022). All five training jobs show loss convergence within the expected range (0.145 to 0.169), with minor fluctuations across epochs: a pattern typical of LoRA fine-tuning where the low-rank adaptation converges quickly given the small number of trainable parameters. Training speed of approximately 6.8 seconds per step on an A100 is reasonable for image-conditioned diffusion model fine-tuning.

However, the LoRA inference results (Section 4.11) reveal that successful training convergence does not translate to improved generation quality. The LoRA-adapted models produce marginally higher PSNR (+0.13 dB) but worse SSIM (0.487 vs. 0.510) and LPIPS (0.487 vs. 0.458), and a lower CLIP Score (21.25 vs. 21.72). More critically, the downstream trajectory impact worsens: the mean ADE delta increases from +0.16 m to +0.61 m, a 3.8× degradation. This result is unexpected given the assumption that domain-specific adaptation should improve generation fidelity. The most likely explanation is that the LoRA training datasets contained false positive samples. The Qwen 2.5-VL model used to caption the SAM 2-segmented road regions may have produced inaccurate or imprecise descriptions, meaning the LoRA adapters were trained on mislabelled image-caption pairs. Training on incorrect associations would naturally degrade rather than improve the model's generation capability. A secondary explanation is that the LoRA adapters, trained on static first-frame images, overfit to the static domain and produce outputs that are subtly less compatible with CogVideoX's temporal propagation: an interaction effect that single-frame evaluation during training would not detect. The finding that CLIP Score actually decreases with LoRA is consistent with both explanations: if the training data contains mislabelled samples, the adapted model learns incorrect text-image associations, producing content that is visually different but not more semantically aligned with the text prompt.

The ProPainter results reported in (Zhou et al., 2023), specifically a +1.46 dB PSNR improvement over E2FGVI, illustrate how far reconstruction-focused methods have come, but these methods operate in a fundamentally different regime from what this thesis attempts. They cannot generate novel semantic content from text, which means they cannot produce lane markings that were not present in the original video. The comparison reinforces the point made in Chapter 2: flow-based video inpainting methods are designed for reconstruction, and the lane marking generation problem requires generative capability that only diffusion-based approaches currently provide.


5.8. Methodological Limitations

Chapter 3, Section 3.9.2 identified the principal methodological limitations of the pipeline design. This section discusses how those limitations manifest in the experimental results and what they mean for the interpretation of the findings.

The full-road mask strategy is perhaps the most consequential limitation. By replacing the entire visible road surface in every frame, the inpainting model must generate not just lane markings but the entire road texture, surface colour, and any other road features (manhole covers, patches, shadows). This is a substantially harder task than replacing only the lane markings themselves, and it may explain why PSNR and SSIM values are low even for prompts where the VLM confirms that the lane markings are correct: the pixel-level deviation includes the entire road surface, not just the lane content. A lane-specific masking approach, where only the existing lane marking pixels are selected for editing, would likely produce higher pixel-level scores and a more precise evaluation of lane marking generation quality, but it would require a lane detection model (such as CLRerNet) upstream of the inpainting stage, adding complexity and another potential failure point.

The single-camera black-out evaluation protocol is a deliberate trade-off between experimental control and ecological validity. Blacking out all non- target cameras forces Alpamayo to base its trajectory prediction entirely on the edited visual input, which isolates the effect of the lane marking edits but creates an input configuration that no production ADAS system would encounter. In a real multi-camera setup, the influence of any single camera is diluted by information from the others. The trajectory deltas measured in Run 006 therefore represent upper-bound estimates of the effect that lane marking edits would have in a realistic deployment. This is appropriate for a first investigation, it establishes that the edits have an effect at all, but subsequent work should explore the same edits in a multi-camera context to determine the practical magnitude of the effect.

The LoRA inference results (Section 4.11) now provide the comparison between base and domain-adapted FluxFill that was pending during the main Run 006 experiments. Contrary to the initial expectation that domain-specific adapters would improve generation quality, the LoRA-adapted models perform slightly worse on perceptual metrics (SSIM, LPIPS) and substantially worse on downstream trajectory impact (mean ADE delta +0.61 m vs. +0.16 m). This means the base FluxFill results reported in Sections 4.3 through 4.6 represent the better-performing configuration, not a conservative baseline. The implication is that the pipeline's current generation quality is near the ceiling of what can be achieved through LoRA fine-tuning at rank 16 with the available training data, and that further quality improvements will likely require either higher- capacity adaptation (higher LoRA rank, full fine-tuning) or architectural changes to the generation pipeline itself.

The ego-motion confound is a structural feature of the experimental design that cannot be eliminated without modifying the evaluation model itself. The identical ego-motion history fed to both inference passes means that Alpamayo can partially predict the trajectory from motion cues alone, independent of visual input. This attenuates the measured effect of visual edits because some portion of the trajectory prediction is invariant to what the camera sees. The true influence of lane marking changes on driving behaviour is likely larger than what the minADE delta captures. However, this attenuation applies equally to all experiments, so relative comparisons between prompts remain valid even if the absolute magnitudes are underestimates.

Finally, the dataset scope of 100 videos from a single camera view provides meaningful statistical observations, the consistency of patterns across five prompts and two variants is strong evidence that the findings are not artefacts of a few unusual videos, but it does not constitute proof of generalisation. The PhysicalAI-AV dataset reflects specific recording conditions (weather, lighting, geography, road types), and the pipeline's performance on fundamentally different driving environments has not been tested. The 100-video sample was selected deterministically (first five videos per chunk, chunks 0 through 19), which avoids selection bias within the dataset but does not address the question of whether the dataset itself is representative of the broader driving population (cf. NVIDIA, 2024).


5.9. Transferability and Generalisability

The question of whether these findings generalise beyond the specific conditions of Run 006 can be considered along three dimensions: the pipeline architecture, the experimental observations, and the evaluation methodology.

The pipeline architecture is highly transferable by design. Each stage is containerised, accepts standardised inputs (video files, binary masks, text prompts) and produces standardised outputs. Replacing the driving dataset with footage from a different camera, geographic region or weather condition requires no architectural changes, only new input data. The geometric prior for segmentation (bottom-half seed points) assumes a front-facing camera with the road in the lower portion of the frame, which holds for virtually all dashcam and autonomous driving camera configurations but would not apply to, say, overhead or rear-facing cameras. For such configurations, the seed point strategy would need to be adjusted, though the rest of the pipeline would remain unchanged.

The modularity extends to the choice of models. SAM 2 could be replaced by a future segmentation model that produces temporally consistent binary masks. FluxFill could be swapped for a more capable image inpainting model: or a domain-specific variant trained with LoRA adapters, as the concurrent training work in this thesis anticipates. CogVideoX/VideoPainter could be replaced by next-generation video diffusion models with improved temporal modelling or higher resolution. The Alpamayo evaluation could be substituted with a different VLA model or even a different type of downstream task (object detection, lane detection) to measure functional impact. The key abstraction, segment, edit, evaluate, is independent of the specific model choices.

The experimental observations are more cautiously transferable. The finding that solid-line prompts produce higher semantic quality than dashed-line prompts is likely to hold for any general-purpose diffusion model, because the underlying reason (solid patterns are geometrically simpler and more prevalent in training data) is not specific to FluxFill or this dataset. Similarly, the observation that CLIP Score differentiates lane marking types while pixel-level metrics do not is a property of the metrics themselves and should generalise to other text-guided editing tasks. However, the specific numeric values, such as CLIP Scores of 19 to 23, improvement rates of 40 to 52 percent, and mean deltas under 0.3 metres, are tied to the particular models, dataset and evaluation protocol used here and should not be assumed to hold with different configurations.

The evaluation methodology, using a VLA trajectory prediction model as a functional assessment tool for video inpainting, is perhaps the most broadly transferable contribution. The principle that generative edits in autonomous driving data should be evaluated not just by how they look but by how they affect driving behaviour applies regardless of the specific inpainting model, evaluation model or driving scenario. The dual-pass protocol (original versus edited, with controlled camera isolation) can be applied to any video editing task where a downstream prediction model exists. This methodology could be extended to other editing targets beyond lane markings, road signs, traffic lights, pedestrians, and to other downstream tasks beyond trajectory prediction: lane keeping, emergency braking, intersection navigation.

The limiting factor for generalisability is the reliance on specific foundation models that are themselves rapidly evolving. SAM 2, CogVideoX and Alpamayo represent the state of the art at the time of this research, but the pace of development in foundation models means that successors with substantially different characteristics are likely to appear within months. The pipeline is designed to accommodate such replacements, but the quantitative findings are snapshots of what these particular models achieve at this particular point in time. The methodological framework, specifically the four pillars described in Section 2.9, is intended to outlast the specific model choices.


5.10. LoRA Fine-Tuning: Expectations vs. Results

The LoRA fine-tuning effort was motivated by the hypothesis that domain- specific adaptation of the FluxFill inpainting model would improve lane marking generation quality, in particular, that LoRA-adapted models would produce lane markings with better semantic accuracy and higher VLM pass rates. The inference results from Section 4.11 provide a comprehensive test of this hypothesis, and the outcome is unambiguously negative.

On inpainting quality, LoRA adaptation produces a negligible improvement in PSNR (+0.13 dB) but meaningful degradations in SSIM (0.487 vs. 0.510) and LPIPS (0.487 vs. 0.458). The CLIP Score decreases from 21.72 to 21.25, indicating that text-video alignment is slightly worse, not better. Temporal consistency is virtually unchanged (0.985 vs. 0.986). These results suggest that LoRA fine-tuning at rank 16, trained on static first-frame images, does not transfer effectively to the full video editing pipeline where the generated first frame must be temporally propagated by CogVideoX.

On caption refinement, LoRA does not address the dashed-pattern failure mode. Prompt p5 achieves a pass rate of only 30 percent with LoRA (6/20), compared with 49 percent without LoRA (49/100). For solid-line prompts, LoRA pass rates (85–100%) are comparable to the base model (89–100%). The VLM quality gate is not the bottleneck for solid-line prompts under either configuration, and LoRA does not help where the bottleneck does exist (dashed patterns).

On trajectory impact, the most striking finding is the 3.8× increase in mean ADE degradation: +0.61 m with LoRA vs. +0.16 m without. This counterintuitive result warrants careful interpretation. The degradation is not uniformly distributed: the median ADE delta across LoRA prompts ranges from −0.00 to +0.08 m, which is close to zero and comparable to the base experiments. The elevated mean is driven by a small number of outlier clips (approximately 6 of 100) where LoRA-edited videos cause ADE degradation exceeding 5 metres. This suggests that LoRA-adapted FluxFill produces outputs that, for certain driving scenes, interact pathologically with the Alpamayo trajectory model: perhaps because the adapted model introduces subtle visual artefacts or textures that the VLA interprets as confusing navigational cues.

Four possible explanations for the negative LoRA outcome merit consideration:

First, and most likely, false positives in the training data. The LoRA training datasets were assembled using a fully automated five-stage pipeline: (1) video discovery from GCS chunk directories, (2) FFmpeg frame extraction at six fixed positions per video, (3) SAM 2 road mask generation in single-frame predictor mode, (4) Qwen 2.5-VL-7B caption generation with a structured JSON prompt requesting lane_marking_count, lane_marking_pattern and lane_marking_color classification, and (5) normalised caption assembly into the format "road with {count} {colour} {pattern} lane markings." The Qwen model receives a system prompt (data_generation.py, line 1062) instructing it to output "unknown" for all fields if the road is not clear or lane markings are not visible. However, this safeguard only catches cases where the model is uncertain: it does not catch confident misclassifications. If the Qwen model labels a faded double yellow marking as "single solid white" with high confidence, the sample passes both the VLM and the downstream REQUIRE_CLEAR_ROAD filter, entering the training set as a false positive.

The structural evidence for this hypothesis is strong. The filtered training datasets show a heavily skewed distribution: double_yellow_solid contains 6,493 images (48.6% of all filtered samples) while single_yellow_solid contains only 372 (2.8%). This imbalance partly reflects real-world marking prevalence, but the ratio seems implausible for European roads where single yellow markings, while less common than white, should not be 17.5 times rarer than double yellow markings. This suggests systematic misclassification, where the VLM is biased toward reporting "double" for certain road geometries or camera angles. Training a LoRA adapter on such mislabelled data teaches the model to associate incorrect visual patterns with lane marking descriptions, which would directly explain why the adapted model produces outputs that are perceptually worse (lower SSIM, higher LPIPS) and cause greater trajectory degradation than the base model. The base FluxFill model, by contrast, relies on its broad pretraining distribution and is not contaminated by domain-specific mislabelling.

The fact that LoRA performance is consistently worse across all five prompts, including those with large training sets (double yellow solid: 6,493 images) and small training sets (single yellow solid: 372 images) alike, is consistent with a systemic data quality issue rather than a data scale or model capacity problem. A manual audit of the training datasets to quantify the false positive rate was not conducted within the scope of this thesis but would be an essential first step before any future LoRA training attempt.

Second, the training-inference domain gap. LoRA adapters were trained on static single-frame images, optimising for a flow matching loss on individual frames. However, at inference time, the LoRA-generated first frame is consumed by CogVideoX for temporal propagation. Any subtle differences in the LoRA output's texture or colour distribution, even if they appear correct to a human observer, may interact poorly with CogVideoX's latent space encoding. The training objective does not account for this downstream compatibility.

Third, the training data scale. The smallest LoRA training set (single white dashed, 373 images) may be insufficient for the model to learn robust dashed- pattern generation. However, the largest training set (double yellow solid, 6,493 images) also shows no quality improvement over the base model, suggesting that data scale alone is not the limiting factor: though this observation is confounded by the data quality concern described above.

Fourth, the LoRA rank capacity. A rank of 16 adds approximately 4 million trainable parameters to a model with billions. This low-rank bottleneck may be insufficient to capture the domain-specific knowledge needed for lane marking generation, particularly for the geometric precision required by dashed patterns. Higher ranks (32, 64) or full fine-tuning could be explored in future work, at the cost of increased compute and potential overfitting.

The prompt adherence analysis (Section 4.11.7) provides additional evidence for the data quality hypothesis. The aggregate VLM pass rate drops from 88% (base) to 80% (LoRA), meaning that LoRA-generated videos less frequently produce lane markings matching the requested attributes. The CLIP Score declines by 0.47 points on average. These prompt adherence metrics directly quantify how well the LoRA inference output conforms to the text prompt, and both measures confirm that the base model is more faithful to the prompt than the LoRA-adapted model.

This prompt adherence gap has cascading implications for the trajectory analysis. When the generated lane markings deviate from the prompt, for instance, producing faded or incorrectly coloured lines when crisp white solid lines were requested, the Alpamayo VLA model responds to what it actually sees rather than what was intended. The 8% aggregate drop in VLM pass rate with LoRA means that approximately 8 additional videos per 100 contain semantically incorrect lane markings, introducing uncontrolled variation into the trajectory analysis. The 3.8× larger mean ADE shift with LoRA (+0.61 m vs. +0.16 m) is therefore partially attributable to the VLA reacting to unintended visual stimuli: a consequence of poor prompt adherence, which itself traces back to false positives in the LoRA training data.

The degradation is most pronounced for prompt p5 (single dashed white), which shows the largest CLIP Score decline (−0.70 points) alongside the largest VLM pass rate drop (−19 percentage points, from 49% to 30%). This consistency across both metrics confirms that LoRA fine-tuning exacerbates rather than resolves the dashed-pattern generation problem identified in Section 5.3. The base FluxFill model already struggles with dashed patterns, and the LoRA adaptation, trained partly on potentially mislabelled dashed-line samples from the automated captioning pipeline, makes the generation even less reliable. This reinforces the conclusion that the dashed-pattern failure is a fundamental limitation of the generation architecture rather than a training data scarcity issue that domain-specific fine-tuning could overcome.

The practical implication for the thesis is that the base FluxFill model is the recommended configuration for the pipeline. LoRA fine-tuning, as implemented, adds training cost (32 GPU-hours) without improving, and potentially degrading, both prompt adherence and downstream functional impact. The most probable root cause is training data contamination from imprecise Qwen VLM captions, which means the negative result reflects a data curation failure rather than a fundamental limitation of the LoRA approach itself. This finding is itself a valuable contribution: it demonstrates that automated training data generation via VLM captioning requires rigorous quality validation before use in fine-tuning, and that evaluation must extend beyond single-frame quality metrics to capture prompt adherence, temporal propagation effects, and downstream task performance. Future work should prioritise a manual audit of the LoRA training datasets to quantify false positive rates, followed by a retrained LoRA experiment using only verified-correct training samples. Additionally, a dedicated prompt adherence benchmark, combining VLM semantic judgement with CLIP-based continuous scoring, should be established as a standard evaluation gate before any LoRA-adapted model is deployed in the production pipeline.
