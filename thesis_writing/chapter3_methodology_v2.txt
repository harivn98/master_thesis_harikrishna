3. METHODOLOGY

The previous chapters established why lane marking diversity matters for autonomous driving systems (Chapter 1) and traced the research landscape from which the component technologies emerged (Chapter 2). The present chapter shifts from theory to practice. It describes, in sufficient detail for independent replication, how the pipeline was designed, how each stage was implemented, how the experiments were configured, and where the boundaries of the methodology lie.

The five research gaps identified in Chapter 1, the absence of video inpainting systems adapted for lane marking attributes, the lack of a dual-model architecture separating first-frame precision from temporal propagation, the absence of automated prompt refinement for inpainting, the reliance on pixel-level metrics rather than functional driving evaluation, and the engineering gap of chaining heterogeneous models into a single reproducible workflow, each translate into a concrete methodological challenge. The design described below addresses all five.


3.1. Research Design Overview

The overall approach is experimental. The pipeline takes real driving videos as input, automatically segments the road surface, replaces the lane markings with text-specified alternatives through generative video inpainting, and then runs a trajectory prediction model on both the original and edited videos to measure whether the edits change predicted driving behaviour. This chain of operations is divided into three stages, each implemented as an independent containerised service connected through shared cloud storage.

The decision to separate the pipeline into discrete stages rather than build a monolithic system was driven by practical constraints. The three foundation models, namely SAM 2 (cf. Ravi et al., 2024) for segmentation, VideoPainter (cf. Bian et al., 2025) for inpainting, and Alpamayo (cf. NVIDIA et al., 2025) for trajectory prediction, have incompatible dependency stacks. SAM 2 and VideoPainter run on Python 3.10 with PyTorch 2.1 and CUDA 11.8, while Alpamayo requires Python 3.12 with PyTorch 2.8, CUDA 12.1, and flash-attention (cf. Dao et al., 2022). Attempting to install all three into a single Python environment proved infeasible during early development, with dependency conflicts between transformer library versions and CUDA toolkits causing persistent build failures. Containerisation resolved this problem. Each model runs in its own Docker image with pinned dependencies, and data flows between stages through well-defined file formats on Google Cloud Storage.

The pipeline supports several execution modes, namely full end-to-end processing (all three stages in sequence), partial execution (any contiguous subset of stages, reusing outputs from a previous run), and standalone operation of individual stages for debugging or incremental development. Seven workflow variants are defined in the master orchestrator to cover these combinations.


3.2. Data Source and Sample Selection

3.2.1. The PhysicalAI-AV Dataset

All experiments use video data from the NVIDIA PhysicalAI-Autonomous-Vehicles dataset (cf. NVIDIA et al., 2025), hosted on HuggingFace and mirrored on Google Cloud Storage. This dataset comprises multi-camera driving recordings from instrumented vehicles operating in real traffic. Each recording includes synchronised video from seven cameras, front wide (120-degree field of view), front telephoto (30-degree), cross-left (120-degree), cross-right (120-degree), rear-left (70-degree), rear-right (70-degree), and rear telephoto (30-degree), along with ego-motion data (positions and rotations) at regular time intervals.

The dataset is organised in Google Cloud Storage as 200 chunk folders (chunk_0000 through chunk_0199), each containing multiple MP4 video files named with a UUID and camera identifier, for example, 25534c8d-4d02-463a-84c9-dad015f320ac.camera_front_tele_30fov.mp4.

3.2.2. Camera Selection

Only the front telephoto camera (camera_front_tele_30fov, 30-degree field of view) is used for video editing. This camera was selected for three reasons. First, the narrow field of view provides a more detailed view of the road surface and lane markings ahead, which is where inpainting precision matters most. Second, lane markings are visually most prominent and geometrically most regular in a forward-looking telephoto view, where perspective convergence makes them appear as clear, relatively straight lines. Third, the Alpamayo VLA model processes all seven cameras but responds most strongly to the front-facing views for trajectory prediction, making the telephoto camera a natural choice for isolating the effect of visual edits on driving behaviour.

3.2.3. Sample Size and Selection

For the main experimental run (Run 006), 100 videos were selected from chunks 0 through 19 of the dataset, with up to five videos drawn from each chunk. The selection was deterministic. Videos were listed in lexicographic order within each chunk, and the first five per chunk were taken. No manual filtering based on scene content was applied. The sample includes highway driving, urban roads, and varied lighting conditions, providing a representative cross-section of the dataset's content.

The choice of 100 videos balances statistical power against computational cost. Each video is processed with five different lane marking prompts in two processing variants, yielding 1,000 edited videos. Each edited video requires a full Alpamayo inference pass (approximately 30 seconds on an A100 GPU) plus a baseline inference pass on the original, bringing the total trajectory evaluation compute to roughly 2,000 inference passes. At the VideoPainter stage, each video takes a median of 7.6 minutes, so 1,000 videos require approximately 130 GPU-hours. The total experimental run consumes roughly 200 GPU-hours across all three stages.


3.3. Stage 1: Road Segmentation with SAM 2

The first pipeline stage takes raw driving videos and produces binary masks identifying the road surface in every frame. These masks serve as the input to the inpainting stage, telling the generative model where to place lane markings and where to preserve the original content.

3.3.1. Model Configuration

The segmentation uses SAM 2.1 (cf. Ravi et al., 2024) with the Hiera-Large checkpoint (305 million parameters), operating in Video Object Segmentation (VOS) mode. VOS mode is critical for this application because it propagates masks from an initial seed frame across all subsequent frames using SAM 2's streaming memory architecture, ensuring temporal consistency. The alternative, running the image predictor independently on each frame, was tested during development and produced masks that flickered between frames, with occasional frames where the road was segmented incorrectly or not at all. VOS mode eliminated this problem.

GPU precision is configured adaptively based on hardware capability. On Ampere-generation GPUs (compute capability 8.0 or higher, including the A100), the model runs in bfloat16 with TF32 enabled for matrix multiplications. On older Turing-generation GPUs (T4), it falls back to float16. This adaptive precision achieves roughly 1.5 to 2 times the inference speed of float32 without measurable quality degradation.

3.3.2. Frame Extraction

Frames are extracted from each input video at a fixed rate of 8 frames per second using FFmpeg's fps filter, regardless of the source video's native frame rate (which varies across the dataset, typically between 20 and 30 fps). SAM 2 extracts a maximum of 100 frames per video for the main experiments (150 for the data generation pipeline). Of these 100 frames, VideoPainter processes only the first 49, which is the native clip length of CogVideoX-5B (cf. Yang et al., 2024), corresponding to approximately 6.1 seconds of footage at 8 fps. The remaining frames retain their segmentation masks for potential future use but are not inpainted in the current pipeline.

The choice of 8 fps was not arbitrary. CogVideoX-5B, the video diffusion backbone in the inpainting stage, expects uniformly spaced input frames. By extracting at the same rate that CogVideoX will later process, no resampling is needed between stages. The 8 fps rate also provides sufficient temporal resolution for SAM 2's memory mechanism to maintain mask coherence. The inter-frame object displacement at this rate is large enough for the model to observe meaningful motion, but small enough to avoid tracking failures.

3.3.3. Automatic Road Initialisation: The Bottom-Half Point Grid

A core design decision was how to prompt SAM 2 to identify the road surface without manual annotation. The approach used is a fixed six-point grid positioned exclusively in the bottom half of the frame.

    Point 1: (0.50, 0.65)   : upper road boundary
    Point 2: (0.35, 0.75)   : mid-road, left of centre
    Point 3: (0.65, 0.75)   : mid-road, right of centre
    Point 4: (0.25, 0.80)   : wide road, left
    Point 5: (0.75, 0.80)   : wide road, right
    Point 6: (0.50, 0.85)   : bottom centre (reference point)

Coordinates are normalised to the frame dimensions (0.0 to 1.0 in each axis). All six points carry a foreground label (label = 1) in the SAM 2 prompt. The underlying geometric insight is straightforward. In any front-facing vehicle camera, the road surface always occupies the lower portion of the frame. The vanishing point sits near the vertical centre, and the area below it is predominantly road. By constraining all seed points to this region, the prompt reliably hits road surface regardless of scene content, including highway, urban intersection, or rural road.

Point 6 at (0.50, 0.85) serves a dual purpose. As a seed point, it contributes to the initial segmentation. As the reference point for connected-component filtering (described below), it anchors the post-processing pipeline to the correct road region. Its position, bottom centre of the frame, directly beneath the ego vehicle, is the single most geometrically certain road location in any dashcam view.

Points are applied only to the first frame. SAM 2's memory mechanism then propagates the resulting mask through all subsequent frames automatically, maintaining temporal coherence across the entire video.

3.3.4. Four-Stage Morphological Post-Processing

SAM 2's raw output masks frequently contain artefacts such as disconnected segments from sidewalks or medians that the model mistakenly merges with the road, small noise regions, and internal holes where lane markings, shadows, or road patches interrupt the road surface. A four-stage pipeline transforms the raw output into a single, clean, hole-free binary mask suitable for inpainting.

Stage 1: Morphological Opening. An elliptical kernel of size 7 x 7 is used to apply morphological opening (erosion followed by dilation). This disconnects thin bridges between the road and adjacent regions, such as sidewalks or parking areas that SAM 2 occasionally merges, and removes small isolated noise blobs.

Stage 2: Connected-Component Filtering with Reference Point. After opening, connected-component analysis identifies all distinct contiguous regions in the mask. The component containing the reference point at (width / 2, height x 0.85), the bottom centre, is retained, and all other components are discarded. This step eliminates disconnected segments such as medians, sidewalks, sky artefacts, or any other regions that are not part of the road directly ahead of the vehicle. As a fallback, if the reference point happens to fall on background (a rare edge case), the largest component is kept instead.

Stage 3: Morphological Closing. An elliptical kernel of size 9 x 9, applied with two iterations, performs morphological closing (dilation followed by erosion). This fills narrow gaps within the road component, for example, where lane markings or shadows create thin breaks in the mask, without expanding the overall boundary.

Stage 4: Flood-Fill Hole Elimination. The mask is inverted so that the background becomes foreground. Starting from the corner at pixel (0, 0), the external background is flood-filled to zero. After flood-filling, any remaining foreground pixels in the inverted mask are internal holes surrounded entirely by road. These holes are merged back into the original mask, producing a single continuous, hole-free road segment.

The reasoning behind requiring hole-free masks is specific to the inpainting stage. VideoPainter's CogVideoX model treats the white mask region as the area to regenerate. If the mask contains internal holes (from lane markings, shadows, or pavement patches), the model would preserve those pixels unchanged while editing the surrounding road, creating visible discontinuities. A single contiguous mask ensures the entire road surface is available for coherent lane marking generation.

Post-processing is parallelised across frames using a thread pool with up to eight workers. Because OpenCV's morphological operations release the Python Global Interpreter Lock, this achieves genuine parallelism and reduces post-processing time by roughly an order of magnitude compared to serial execution.

3.3.5. Output Formats

The segmentation stage produces three output formats.

First, binary PNG masks for each frame, where pixel value 0 means "keep original" and pixel value 255 means "inpaint this region." These are the format that VideoPainter's inference script expects.

Second, a compressed NumPy archive (NPZ) containing all masks for the video in a single file. This format is 10 to 50 times smaller than the equivalent set of PNG files and is the primary format used for data transfer between stages.

Third, visualisation overlay videos showing the segmented region highlighted in green on each frame, used for manual quality inspection.

Additionally, the stage creates a VideoPainter-compatible folder structure containing a transcoded video (at 8 fps), the NPZ mask archive, and a metadata CSV recording the video's dimensions, frame count, and detected frame rate. This folder structure is uploaded to Google Cloud Storage in a format that the inpainting stage can consume directly, with no intermediate reformatting.

3.3.6. Relationship to the Upstream SAM 2 Repository

Clarity about the boundary between the upstream SAM 2 library and the thesis-specific code is essential for understanding the technical contribution. A line-by-line comparison was performed between the official Facebook Research SAM 2 repository (github.com/facebookresearch/sam2, commit 2b90b9f) and the local version used in this thesis. The results are unambiguous. The SAM 2 core library is entirely untouched.

Every file under the sam2/ Python package, including sam2/build_sam.py (model construction), sam2/sam2_video_predictor.py (1,224 lines, including the SAM2VideoPredictorVOS class with torch.compile optimisations), sam2/sam2_image_predictor.py (467 lines), all modelling code (SAM2Base, memory encoder, memory attention, position encoding, Hiera backbone), all configuration YAML files, the CUDA connected-component implementation, and all utility modules, is byte-identical to the upstream repository. The setup.py, pyproject.toml, tools/vos_inference.py, backend.Dockerfile, training code, notebooks, and dataset utilities are also unchanged. No line of Meta's SAM 2 code was added, removed, or modified.

The thesis contribution resides entirely in a new application layer built around the unmodified SAM 2 library. This application layer comprises approximately 5,390 lines of novel code across 12 new files and 1 rewritten file, none of which exist in the upstream repository. What follows is not merely an inventory of these files but an analysis of what each component contributes, why it was necessary, and what its presence implies for the research methodology.

3.3.6.1. The Road Segmentation Engine (process_videos_sam2.py)

At 1,122 lines, process_videos_sam2.py is the single file that transforms SAM 2 from a general-purpose foundation model into a domain-specific road segmentation system. SAM 2 was released as a promptable model. It can segment any object given appropriate prompts, but it has no inherent knowledge of roads, driving scenes, or the spatial conventions of lane marking regions. This file bridges that gap.

The significance lies in what it encodes, namely a complete set of domain assumptions about autonomous driving video data. The six-point grid prompt strategy (Section 3.3.3), the bottom-half spatial restriction, the morphological post-processing pipeline (dilation, hole filling, contour filtering), the frame rate detection and adaptive extraction logic, the per-video temporary directory management, and the parallel GCS upload system are all decisions that embody knowledge about what road regions look like, how driving videos are structured, and how masks must be formatted for downstream inpainting.

The inference from this file's existence is that SAM 2, despite being the most capable promptable segmentation model available, cannot be applied to the road segmentation task out of the box. A substantial engineering layer is required to (a) formulate the right prompts for every frame, (b) enforce spatial priors about where roads appear, (c) clean the raw mask output to produce inpainting-grade masks, and (d) manage the data flow between cloud storage, GPU processing, and downstream pipeline stages. This is a practical finding about the gap between foundation model capability and domain application, a gap that required over a thousand lines of code to bridge.

3.3.6.2. The Training Data Factory (data_generation.py)

The data_generation.py file (1,432 lines) is the largest novel file and represents perhaps the most architecturally significant contribution. It orchestrates two independent foundation models, namely SAM 2 for mask generation and Qwen2.5-VL-7B for visual language captioning, in a single automated pipeline that produces (image, mask, caption) training triplets from raw driving videos.

What this file implies is that the thesis contribution extends beyond inference-time pipeline construction into the creation of an automated training data generation system. The traditional approach to fine-tuning a generative model like FluxFill would require manual annotation. A human operator would segment road regions, write descriptive captions for each image, and assemble the training CSV. This file eliminates that manual step entirely. It downloads MP4 files from GCS, extracts frames at fixed positions, generates masks using SAM 2's image predictor mode (not VOS mode, since temporal consistency is unnecessary for independent training images), produces structured captions via the VLM, and assembles the complete training dataset, all without human intervention.

The practical consequence is scale. The automated pipeline processed 200 video chunks and produced over 10,000 training samples. Generating this volume of annotated data manually would require weeks of human labour; the automated system completes it in hours. This scalability is what makes the LoRA fine-tuning experiments (Section 3.5) feasible within the time constraints of a master thesis.

The file also demonstrates a non-trivial systems engineering pattern, namely per-device model singletons. Both SAM 2 and Qwen2.5-VL-7B are loaded once per GPU and shared across threads via thread-safe singleton accessors. This design exploits the fact that both models release Python's Global Interpreter Lock during CUDA inference, enabling genuine parallelism within a single process. The implication is that the pipeline's throughput is limited by GPU compute, not by Python's threading model. This design choice that would be invisible in a simpler sequential implementation but is essential for processing thousands of videos in reasonable wall-clock time.

3.3.6.3. The Semantic Filter (filter_fluxfill_dataset.py)

The filter_fluxfill_dataset.py file (751 lines) converts the raw generated dataset, containing a mix of all lane marking types, into attribute-specific training subsets. It parses the normalised captions produced by data_generation.py using a regular expression that extracts count, colour, and pattern attributes, and filters samples by any combination of these dimensions.

The significance of this file is methodological. The thesis investigates whether lane marking type affects autonomous driving trajectory prediction (Chapter 4). To conduct this investigation, separate LoRA adapters must be trained for each marking type (single white solid, double yellow solid, etc.). This requires not just a large dataset but a large dataset that can be precisely partitioned by semantic attributes. The filtering script is the mechanism that enables attribute-controlled experimentation. Without it, the five-way LoRA comparison in Section 3.5 would not be possible.

The REQUIRE_CLEAR_ROAD quality gate, which rejects any sample whose VLM caption contains "unknown" for any attribute, reveals a deliberate trade-off between dataset size and label confidence. By discarding ambiguous samples, the pipeline sacrifices quantity for precision. This is a defensible choice given that LoRA training with noisy labels could teach the adapter to reproduce the wrong lane marking type, undermining the entire experimental design. The filtered dataset sizes (ranging from 372 to 6,493 images across the five types) reflect the real-world distribution of lane marking types in the PhysicalAI-AV dataset, which itself is a finding. Yellow and dashed markings are genuinely scarce in the source data.

3.3.6.4. Cloud Orchestration (workflow_sam2.py and supporting workflows)

The workflow_sam2.py file (444 lines), together with workflow_framesextraction.py (344 lines) and workflow_datasplitting.py (306 lines), constitutes the cloud orchestration layer. These files define HLX workflow graphs that manage GPU node allocation, GCS FuseBucket mounting for checkpoint and data access, container image selection, and job parameterisation.

What these files imply about the research methodology is that the pipeline was designed from the outset for cloud-scale execution, not for single-machine experimentation. The FuseBucket mount pattern, where the SAM 2.1 checkpoint (890 MB) is accessed via a FUSE filesystem mount rather than copied to each node, is an infrastructure optimisation that becomes meaningful only when running dozens of parallel jobs. The workflow files encode the knowledge of how to translate a conceptual pipeline ("segment these 100 videos") into concrete cloud resource requests (GPU type, shared memory allocation, container image, environment variables, GCS prefixes).

The inference is that reproducibility at this scale requires explicit infrastructure-as-code. Without these workflow definitions, replicating the experiments would require manually configuring cloud nodes, mounting storage, setting environment variables, and launching jobs, a process that is both error-prone and undocumented. The workflow files serve as executable documentation of the experimental infrastructure.

3.3.6.5. The Docker Rewrite and Its Implications

The only upstream file that was replaced (not modified but rewritten entirely) is docker-compose.yaml. The original defined a two-service web demonstration application: a React frontend for interactive mask annotation and a Gunicorn backend API server exposing SAM 2 through a REST interface. The local version defines a single batch processing container with access to all available GPUs, 16 GB of shared memory, and the full repository mounted as a working directory.

This rewrite is small (30 lines) but its implications are substantial. It represents a fundamental change in how SAM 2 is used, from an interactive tool where a human operator clicks on objects to annotate them, to an automated batch system where a programmatic prompt strategy processes hundreds of videos without human intervention. The original docker-compose.yaml assumes a single user, a single image, and a web browser; the rewritten version assumes no user, hundreds of videos, and a GPU cluster. This shift, from interactive demo to automated production pipeline, is the operational core of the thesis contribution and explains why the rest of the application layer (the 5,360 remaining lines) was necessary.

The 16 GB shared memory allocation (shm_size) is itself a technical decision with implications. PyTorch's DataLoader uses shared memory for inter-process communication when num_workers > 0, and the SAM 2 video predictor's propagation phase creates large intermediate tensors (per-frame mask logits for up to 100 frames at the input resolution). The default Docker shared memory of 64 MB would cause silent failures during long video processing.

3.3.6.6. Architectural Separation and Its Consequences

The decision to leave the SAM 2 core entirely unmodified, zero lines changed across all 10,500+ lines of Meta's code, was architecturally deliberate and has three consequences for the research.

First, reproducibility. Any researcher can clone the official SAM 2 repository at commit 2b90b9f, overlay the 12 novel files, and reproduce the exact pipeline used in this thesis. There is no risk of undocumented patches, monkey-patches, or version-specific hacks buried in the model code. The boundary between "what Meta built" and "what this thesis built" is unambiguous.

Second, upgradeability. Because the application layer interacts with SAM 2 exclusively through three public API methods, init_state() (which accepts a directory of JPEG frames and returns an inference state object), add_new_points_or_box() (which registers point or box prompts for a specific frame and object), and propagate_in_video() (which yields per-frame mask logits as a generator), the pipeline can adopt future SAM 2 versions or entirely different segmentation models without modifying process_videos_sam2.py, provided the replacement exposes the same three-method interface. This is not a theoretical benefit. During development, the pipeline was migrated from SAM 2.0 to SAM 2.1 by changing only the checkpoint path and configuration file name, with zero changes to the application code.

Third, attribution clarity. In a thesis that combines multiple foundation models (SAM 2, FluxFill, CogVideoX-5B, Qwen2.5-VL-7B, Alpamayo-R1-10B), clear separation between upstream libraries and novel code is essential for honest attribution. The unmodified SAM 2 core means that the segmentation quality reported in this thesis is entirely attributable to Meta's model; the thesis contribution is the domain-specific prompt strategy, post-processing pipeline, cloud infrastructure, and training data generation system that make that model applicable to the lane marking editing task.

3.3.6.7. Summary of the Technical Contribution

The approximately 5,390 lines of novel code represent a complete application engineering layer that transforms SAM 2 from a general-purpose research prototype into a production-grade, cloud-native road segmentation system. The contribution is not a modification of the model but the construction of everything needed to deploy that model at scale for a specific domain task, including the prompt strategy, the mask post-processing, the training data generation, the semantic filtering, the cloud workflow orchestration, and the containerised deployment infrastructure. The fact that this required nearly half as many lines as the SAM 2 library itself (5,390 versus approximately 10,500) quantifies the engineering effort required to bridge the gap between a foundation model's general capability and a specific research application.


3.4. Stage 2: Video Inpainting with the Dual-Model Architecture

The second pipeline stage generates lane markings in the masked road regions. The inpainting architecture combines two generative models in sequence, namely an image inpainting model (FluxFill) that generates a semantically precise first frame, and a video diffusion model (CogVideoX-5B with the VideoPainter context encoder) that propagates the edit across all remaining frames with temporal consistency.

3.4.1. Why a Dual-Model Architecture

Chapter 2 discussed the conceptual rationale for separating first-frame generation from temporal propagation. Here, the practical reasoning is made explicit. During early experiments, VideoPainter was run in its default configuration, with CogVideoX generating all frames from scratch, conditioned on a text prompt and the masked video. The results were temporally consistent but frequently lacked the semantic precision needed for controlled lane marking attributes. CogVideoX, optimised for temporal coherence across 49-frame clips, would sometimes produce a single white line when a double yellow line was requested, or generate solid markings when dashed ones were specified. The model was not wrong in a visual sense, the generated content looked like plausible road markings, but it did not reliably match the fine-grained textual specification.

FluxFill (cf. Esser et al., 2024), operating on a single image, is far more responsive to text-prompt details. Its rectified flow Transformer backbone, trained specifically for inpainting, can render lane markings with accurate count, colour and pattern when given a sufficiently detailed prompt. But it has no temporal awareness and produces only one frame.

The dual-model architecture exploits each model's strength. FluxFill generates a first frame that captures the intended lane marking attributes with high fidelity. CogVideoX then receives this frame as its conditioning image and propagates the edit temporally, preserving the spatial content that FluxFill established while ensuring smooth frame-to-frame transitions. The result combines FluxFill's semantic precision with CogVideoX's temporal consistency.

3.4.2. Mask Dilation and Feathering

Before inpainting, the binary road masks from Stage 1 undergo two geometric operations. First, morphological dilation with a kernel of 8 pixels expands the mask beyond the precise road boundary. This expansion is deliberate: without it, the transition between generated content and the original background occurs exactly at the road edge, creating visible seams. By dilating the mask, the inpainting model has a buffer zone in which to blend generated content with the surrounding context.

Second, Gaussian blur with a kernel of 4 pixels feathers the mask edges, creating a soft gradient from fully inpainted to fully preserved. This feathering smooths the composite further, eliminating hard boundaries in the final output.

Both parameters (dilation size of 8 pixels, feather size of 4 pixels) were determined empirically through visual inspection of early outputs. Larger dilation values produced cleaner transitions but expanded the edited region into non-road areas; smaller values left visible seams. The chosen values represent a balance that worked consistently across the dataset.

In the main experiments, the keep_masked_pixels flag is disabled. This means FluxFill generates content across the full frame rather than applying a hard composite with the original background pixels outside the dilated mask. The inpainting model's conditioning on the masked input naturally preserves context outside the road region, though minor modifications to non-road areas (sky, buildings, vehicles) are possible. This design was chosen because hard compositing at the mask boundary can introduce visible seams, whereas allowing the diffusion model to blend the full frame produces smoother transitions, particularly at mask edges where the feathering gradient operates.

3.4.3. VLM-Guided Iterative Caption Refinement

Text-guided diffusion models are sensitive to prompt wording. A prompt like "single solid white line on road" may or may not produce the intended result. Minor phrasing differences can cause FluxFill to generate subtly different outputs. To address this, an automated quality assurance loop was implemented using the Qwen2.5-VL-7B vision-language model (cf. Bai et al., 2025).

A technical note on this choice is relevant here. VideoPainter's original evaluation scripts (evaluate/eval_inpainting.py, evaluate/eval_edit.py) use the OpenAI Python client to call GPT-4 through a proprietary API for visual quality assessment. This creates an external dependency on a commercial service. Every evaluation requires an API key, incurs per-call costs, and sends potentially sensitive automotive data to a third-party server. In this thesis, all OpenAI API calls were replaced with a locally running Qwen2.5-VL-7B model (evaluate/metrics.py), which runs entirely within the pipeline's own container on the same GPU. This substitution eliminates the API dependency, makes the pipeline fully self-contained and reproducible without external credentials, and keeps all data within the Mercedes-Benz cloud environment, a practical requirement for industrial ADAS development where data governance is a concern. The same local VLM serves both the caption refinement loop and the first-frame quality evaluation, consolidating what would otherwise be two separate external API integrations into a single on-device model.

The refinement loop works as follows. An initial caption is constructed from the editing instruction, incorporating details about perspective alignment, road texture, lighting and paint appearance. FluxFill generates a candidate first frame using this caption. The candidate frame, together with the original frame and the editing instruction, is sent to Qwen2.5-VL. The VLM evaluates whether the generated lane markings match the specification across five dimensions: lane count (single versus double), colour accuracy (white versus yellow), pattern type (solid versus dashed), perspective alignment with the original road geometry, and preservation of road texture and lighting.

The VLM produces a structured response, either "PASS" (the generated frame matches the specification) or "FAIL" with a revised caption that includes more specific visual details, for example, adding texture descriptors like "crisp painted lines with slight wear" or adjusting spatial descriptions like "centred between road edges, converging at vanishing point." If the verdict is FAIL, FluxFill regenerates the first frame with the revised caption, and the evaluation repeats. The loop terminates when the VLM returns PASS or when the maximum iteration count is reached.

In the main experiments, the maximum iteration count is set to 10 and the VLM sampling temperature is 0.1 (nearly deterministic, to avoid random variation in evaluations). The VLM runs on a separate GPU when two GPUs are available, or on the same GPU with explicit model unloading when only one GPU is present.

3.4.4. Temporal Propagation with CogVideoX

After the first frame is approved (or the iteration limit reached), CogVideoX-5B with the VideoPainter context encoder generates the remaining frames. CogVideoX operates in a compressed latent space through a 3D causal VAE with 4x temporal compression and 8x8 spatial compression, generating 49 frames per clip at 480 x 720 resolution.

In the main experiments, each video is processed as a single 49-frame clip: the native sequence length of CogVideoX. SAM 2 extracts 100 frames at 8 fps, but VideoPainter consumes only the first 49 because CogVideoX produces its best temporal coherence within this window. For longer sequences (such as the 150-frame data generation pipeline), VideoPainter's any-length processing module divides the sequence into overlapping clips with a target region ID resampling mechanism that ensures consistent appearance across clip boundaries.

The key inference parameters are:

    num_inference_steps:     70  (number of denoising steps)
    guidance_scale:          6.0 (classifier-free guidance weight; cf. Ho et al., 2022)
    strength:                1.0 (full inpainting: no blending with noise)
    seed:                    42  (fixed for reproducibility)

These values were inherited from VideoPainter's recommended settings and validated on a small pilot set of videos before the main experiments.

3.4.5. GPU Memory Management

Running three large models (Qwen2.5-VL at 7 billion parameters, FluxFill at approximately 12 billion parameters, and CogVideoX at 5 billion parameters) on a single NVIDIA A100 80 GB GPU required careful memory orchestration. The solution is staged model loading and unloading.

Step 1: Qwen2.5-VL is loaded first, consuming approximately 14 GB of VRAM. The VLM generates or refines the caption for the first frame.

Step 2: Qwen2.5-VL is explicitly unloaded. All model references are deleted, the Python garbage collector runs, and torch.cuda.empty_cache() reclaims the VRAM.

Step 3: FluxFill is loaded, consuming approximately 24 GB. It generates the first frame using the approved caption.

Step 4: FluxFill is unloaded using the same deletion and cache-clearing procedure.

Step 5: CogVideoX and the VideoPainter context encoder are loaded, consuming approximately 35 GB. The video generation proceeds.

This sequential load-unload pattern keeps peak VRAM usage at approximately 40 GB, well within the A100's 80 GB capacity. The overhead of loading and unloading models, approximately 30 seconds per transition, is small relative to the inference time per video (median 7.6 minutes).

The PYTORCH_CUDA_ALLOC_CONF environment variable is set to "expandable_segments:True" to allow dynamic CUDA memory management, reducing fragmentation-related out-of-memory errors during the model transitions.

3.4.6. Evaluation Metrics at the Inpainting Stage

After each video is generated, a suite of quality metrics is computed automatically. These include full-frame and masked-region variants of PSNR, SSIM (cf. Wang et al., 2004), LPIPS (cf. Zhang et al., 2018), MSE, MAE and temporal consistency, plus CLIP Score (cf. Radford et al., 2021) measuring text-video alignment. The formulations of these metrics were described in Chapter 2 (Section 2.8). Here it is sufficient to note that they are computed on GPU tensors with shape [T, C, H, W] using the MetricsCalculator from VideoPainter's evaluation module, and the results are saved as a text file alongside each generated video for post-processing.

A three-level quality classification is applied: EXCELLENT (two or more of PSNR > 40 dB, SSIM > 0.95, LPIPS < 0.1), GOOD (one met), or MODERATE (none met). In the main experiments, all 1,000 videos received a MODERATE classification, which is expected and desired, since the inpainting intentionally changes the entire road surface, producing large pixel-level differences from the original.

3.4.7. Prompt Adherence Evaluation

Beyond pixel-level quality, a critical evaluation dimension is prompt adherence, defined as the degree to which the generated video content matches the text prompt used to instruct the inpainting model. This thesis measures prompt adherence through two complementary mechanisms:

First, CLIP Score provides a continuous quantitative measure of text-video alignment. CLIP Score is computed by encoding the text prompt and each generated video frame through CLIP's joint embedding space, then measuring cosine similarity. Higher CLIP Scores indicate stronger alignment between what was requested and what was produced. Because CLIP operates in a learned semantic space, it captures abstract correspondence (e.g., "yellow" matching yellow-coloured content) rather than exact pixel-level matching.

Second, VLM caption refinement provides a binary semantic judgement. The Qwen 2.5-VL evaluator (Section 3.4.3) inspects the generated first frame against the editing instruction and produces a PASS or FAIL verdict based on five dimensions: lane count (single versus double), colour accuracy (white versus yellow), pattern type (solid versus dashed), perspective alignment, and context preservation. The pass rate across a set of videos quantifies the fraction that achieve full semantic compliance with the prompt.

These two measures capture different aspects of prompt adherence. CLIP Score measures global text-visual coherence and is sensitive to all semantic content in the frame, while VLM caption refinement focuses specifically on the lane marking attributes that the prompt explicitly specifies. Together, they provide a comprehensive assessment of whether the inpainting model, with or without LoRA adaptation, produces content that faithfully represents the requested lane markings. The results of this prompt adherence evaluation are reported in Section 4.4 (base model) and Section 4.10.3 and 4.10.8 (LoRA comparison).


3.4.8. Relationship to the Upstream VideoPainter Repository

The same verification methodology applied to SAM 2 in Section 3.3.6 was applied to the VideoPainter codebase. A file-by-file comparison was performed between the official TencentARC/VideoPainter repository (github.com/TencentARC/VideoPainter) and the local version used in this thesis. Unlike SAM 2, where the upstream code was entirely untouched, the VideoPainter codebase required both new files and targeted modifications to existing files. Understanding which files were changed, and why, is essential for delimiting the thesis contribution from the upstream research.

The upstream VideoPainter repository contains approximately 696 source files (excluding binary assets). Of these, 688 are byte-identical in the local version. Eight files were modified, and thirteen entirely new files were added. No upstream files were deleted. The total volume of novel and modified code is approximately 5,500 lines.

3.4.8.1. The Central Inference Rewrite (infer/edit_bench.py)

The most extensively modified file is infer/edit_bench.py, which grew from 700 lines in the upstream to 1,787 lines, an addition of 1,087 lines. This file is the heart of the VideoPainter editing pipeline, and the modifications constitute the single largest code contribution in the entire thesis.

The upstream edit_bench.py relies on the OpenAI Python client to call GPT-4o through a commercial API for all vision-language tasks, including caption generation, editing instruction interpretation, and quality evaluation. Every inference run requires an API key, incurs per-call costs, and transmits video frames to an external server. In an industrial ADAS development context, where driving data is subject to data governance policies, this external dependency is both a practical and a compliance obstacle.

The thesis replacement eliminates this dependency entirely. All OpenAI API calls are replaced with a locally hosted Qwen2.5-VL-7B model loaded via HuggingFace Transformers. The local model runs on the same GPU (or a dedicated second GPU when available), keeping all data within the pipeline's own container. This substitution required approximately 250 lines of new code for model loading, multi-GPU memory management, device routing, and CPU offload logic.

Beyond the VLM replacement, the file adds six major capabilities that do not exist in the upstream:

First, the dual-model architecture. The upstream edit_bench.py uses only CogVideoX-5B for all frame generation. The modified version inserts FluxFill as a first-frame generator, with a complete integration layer including FluxFillPipeline loading, LoRA adapter support (load_lora_weights and set_adapters), prompt formatting, and result handoff to CogVideoX. This is the code that implements the dual-model architecture described in Section 3.4.1.

Second, the iterative caption refinement loop. Approximately 120 lines implement the VLM-in-the-loop quality assurance cycle described in Section 3.4.3: FluxFill generates a candidate frame, Qwen2.5-VL evaluates it against the editing instruction across five dimensions, and if the verdict is FAIL, a revised caption is produced and the cycle repeats. This loop includes the construction of multi-panel evaluation images (original, mask, and inpainted side by side) to give the VLM full visual context.

Third, two-phase single-GPU execution. Running three large models (Qwen2.5-VL, FluxFill, CogVideoX) on a single A100 80 GB GPU required a two-phase execution strategy. The first phase loads Qwen2.5-VL and FluxFill, generates the refined first frame, saves the result to disk, and unloads both models. The second phase loads CogVideoX and generates the remaining frames from the saved first frame. This staged approach keeps peak VRAM below 40 GB. The implementation includes explicit model unloading (del, gc.collect, torch.cuda.empty_cache) and state serialisation between phases.

Fourth, border-aware masking. A new module (infer/border_utils.py, 154 lines) provides content-aware mask filling using OpenCV's Telea inpainting or Gaussian blur, replacing the upstream's simple black-fill approach. In driving videos, camera movement causes mask boundaries to shift between frames; black- filled regions at these boundaries create visible artifacts. The content-aware approach produces smoother transitions.

Fifth, mask dilation and feathering. The mask post-processing described in Section 3.4.2, dilation with an 8-pixel kernel followed by 4-pixel Gaussian blur feathering, is implemented here, adding approximately 30 lines.

Sixth, domain-specific prompting. Approximately 100 lines of new prompt templates encode knowledge about autonomous driving scenes and lane marking attributes (single versus double, white versus yellow, solid versus dashed), including perspective alignment instructions and road texture preservation directives. These templates replace the upstream's generic editing prompts.

The inference from this file's transformation is that the upstream VideoPainter, while architecturally sound for general video editing, required substantial adaptation for the lane marking domain. The general-purpose CogVideoX-only pipeline lacked the semantic precision needed for controlled attribute editing; the commercial API dependency precluded deployment in data-governed environments; and the single-model architecture could not fit the required model ensemble onto available hardware. Solving these three problems required rewriting the majority of the inference logic.

3.4.8.2. VLM Migration in Supporting Inference Files

Two additional inference files, namely infer/edit.py (641 to 688 lines, +47 lines) and infer/inpaint.py (601 to 665 lines, +64 lines), received the same OpenAI-to-Qwen replacement. Both files gain the Qwen2.5-VL model loader, the local text generation function, and a frame rate calculation fix (the upstream hardcoded fps=8 for video export; the modified version computes the actual content frame rate from the source video's native rate and temporal stride). The inpaint.py file additionally gains FluxFill LoRA loading support, mirroring the LoRA integration in edit_bench.py.

Two files in the vendored Diffusers library were modified to enable LoRA adapter loading for FluxFill:

The evaluate/metrics.py file grew from 903 to 1,125 lines (+222 lines). The upstream version depends on two external services, namely the OpenAI API for text-based quality assessment and the openai/clip PyPI package for CLIP feature extraction. Both are replaced.

3.4.8.8. Summary and Implications

The VideoPainter modifications total approximately 5,500 lines of novel and modified code. Unlike the SAM 2 contribution, where the upstream was entirely untouched, the VideoPainter contribution includes both new application-layer files (approximately 4,200 lines) and targeted modifications to the inference and evaluation code (approximately 1,300 lines across six files).

The modifications fall into four categories, each addressing a specific gap between the upstream research prototype and the requirements of this thesis:

First, VLM independence (approximately 400 lines across four files). Replacing OpenAI API calls with local Qwen2.5-VL eliminates external dependencies, makes the pipeline fully self-contained, and enables deployment in data-governed environments. This is not merely a vendor substitution: it required building a complete local VLM inference stack with GPU memory management, multi-device routing, and model lifecycle control.

Second, the dual-model architecture (approximately 300 lines in edit_bench.py plus 64 lines in Diffusers). Inserting FluxFill as a first-frame generator required integrating a second diffusion pipeline, implementing LoRA adapter loading (including Diffusers library patches), and building the iterative caption refinement loop. This is the methodological core of the thesis: the insight that separating semantic precision (FluxFill) from temporal consistency (CogVideoX) yields better controlled lane marking generation.

Third, production infrastructure (approximately 2,700 lines across workflow files, Dockerfile, scripts). The upstream provides shell scripts for single- machine execution. The thesis requires cloud-scale batch processing of hundreds of videos. Bridging this gap required building complete workflow orchestration, containerisation, and deployment automation.

Fourth, the LoRA training pipeline (approximately 1,450 lines across three files). The upstream VideoPainter does not include any mechanism for domain- specific fine-tuning of the image inpainting model. The thesis adds a complete end-to-end training system: dataset preparation, CSV-based data loading, LoRA configuration and training, checkpoint management, and cloud workflow orchestration.

The fact that 688 of 696 upstream files remain byte-identical confirms that the thesis builds upon the VideoPainter architecture rather than replacing it. The CogVideoX backbone, the context encoder, and the temporal propagation mechanism are used exactly as published. The contribution is the engineering layer that makes this architecture applicable to the lane marking editing domain at production scale.


3.5. Training Data Generation and LoRA Fine-Tuning

To improve FluxFill's lane marking generation beyond the base model's capabilities, a parameter-efficient fine-tuning pipeline based on Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA; cf. Hu et al., 2022) was developed. This section describes how training data was generated, filtered and used to train domain-specific LoRA adapters.

3.5.1. Automated Dataset Generation

The training data generation pipeline is a fully automated system that produces (image, mask, caption) triplets from raw driving videos. The process has five stages.

First, video discovery: the pipeline lists MP4 files in the GCS chunk structure, selecting videos by chunk range and files-per-chunk parameters. An early-stop optimisation terminates the GCS listing as soon as enough files have been collected for the requested slice.

Second, frame extraction: for each video, six frames are extracted at fixed positions (frame numbers 1, 100, 200, 300, 400, 500) using FFmpeg. Extracting multiple frames per video at widely spaced intervals ensures diversity of road geometry and lighting within each video's contribution to the dataset.

Third, SAM 2 mask generation: the image predictor mode (not VOS mode, since temporal consistency is unnecessary for independent training images) generates a binary road mask for each extracted frame using the same bottom-half point grid described in Section 3.3.3.

Fourth, VLM caption generation: each frame is sent to Qwen2.5-VL-7B with a structured prompt requesting identification of the visible lane marking attributes. The VLM extracts three attributes, count (single, double, or unknown), colour (white, yellow, mixed, or unknown), and pattern (solid, dashed, mixed, or unknown), and produces a normalised caption in the format "road with {count} {colour} {pattern} lane markings." This structured format enables downstream attribute-based filtering.

Fifth, dataset assembly: a CSV file is created with columns for image path, mask path, primary prompt, and secondary prompt, with all paths relative to the dataset root for portability. The images and masks are stored in separate subdirectories, and the complete dataset is uploaded to GCS.

The pipeline processes chunks 0 through 99 (half the dataset), yielding over 10,000 training samples. Performance optimisations include per-device model singletons (SAM 2 and Qwen loaded once per GPU and shared across threads), thread-based parallelism (exploiting the fact that both models release the GIL during inference), and parallel GCS uploads using an eight-thread pool.

3.5.2. Semantic Attribute Filtering

The raw generated dataset contains a mix of lane marking types. Training a LoRA adapter specialised for a single lane marking configuration, for example, "single white solid", requires filtering out all non-matching samples. A filtering script parses the normalised captions using a regular expression:

    road with (?P<count>...) (?P<colour>...) (?P<pattern>...) lane markings

This extracts the three attributes from each caption. Filtering is configured by specifying the desired value for each attribute (or "any" to accept all values). An additional quality control flag, REQUIRE_CLEAR_ROAD, rejects any sample whose caption contains "unknown" for any attribute, ensuring that only unambiguously labelled samples enter the training set.

Five filtered datasets are created, one for each lane marking type corresponding to the five experimental prompts:

    1. single_white_solid     ; 1,522 images
    2. double_white_solid     ; 4,612 images
    3. single_yellow_solid    ;   372 images
    4. double_yellow_solid    ; 6,493 images
    5. single_white_dashed    ;   373 images

The uneven distribution reflects the actual prevalence of lane marking types in the PhysicalAI-AV dataset. White solid markings dominate (as expected from data recorded primarily on European roads), while yellow and dashed markings are comparatively scarce, which is precisely the imbalance that motivates this thesis.

3.5.3. LoRA Training Configuration

Five independent LoRA adapters are trained, one per filtered dataset, on separate A100 80 GB GPUs in parallel. The LoRA configuration targets the attention projection layers of FluxFill's Transformer blocks:

    Target modules:              to_q, to_k, to_v, to_out.0
    Rank (r):                    16
    Alpha:                       16
    Dropout:                     0.0

The rank of 16 was chosen as a balance between adapter capacity and overfitting risk. Since some filtered datasets contain fewer than 400 images, a higher rank could memorise the training data rather than learning generalisable lane marking patterns.

Training hyperparameters are identical across all five jobs:

    Optimiser:                   AdamW (cf. Loshchilov et al., 2019; weight decay 0.01)
    Learning rate:               1e-5
    Learning rate schedule:      Cosine annealing
    Warmup steps:                50
    Number of epochs:            5
    Batch size:                  1 (per GPU)
    Gradient accumulation steps: 4 (effective batch size: 4)
    Mixed precision:             bfloat16
    Checkpointing interval:      Every 100 steps
    Seed:                        42

Training speed is approximately 6.8 seconds per step on the A100. The two smallest datasets (single_yellow_solid and single_white_dashed, approximately 370 images each) complete in roughly 53 minutes. The largest (double_yellow_solid, 6,493 images) requires approximately 15 hours. All training losses stabilise by epoch three, with final-epoch average losses in the range 0.145 to 0.165 across the completed jobs.

Before training begins, each job downloads its training data (images and masks) from Google Cloud Storage to local scratch space. Download time scales linearly with dataset size. The single_white_solid dataset (1,522 images plus 1,522 masks) downloads in approximately 3 minutes, while the double_white_solid dataset (4,612 images plus 4,612 masks) requires approximately 12 minutes; 8.5 minutes for images at roughly 100 files per 7.5 seconds and 3.5 minutes for masks at roughly 100 files per 4.7 seconds. This pre-training data staging step is a small fraction of the total training wall time even for the largest datasets.

The resulting LoRA adapter weights are compact, significantly smaller than the base FluxFill model, and can be loaded at inference time with a single function call, enabling rapid switching between lane marking specialisations.

At inference time, LoRA integration is handled entirely within edit_bench.py. The preload_models() function loads the FluxFillPipeline and, if a LoRA checkpoint path is provided, calls load_lora_weights() followed by set_adapters() at the specified scale (default 1.0). The LoRA adapter modifies the attention projection layers (to_q, to_k, to_v, to_out.0) during every denoising step of first-frame generation. The rest of the pipeline, VLM-guided caption refinement, CogVideoX temporal propagation, and Alpamayo trajectory evaluation, proceeds identically to the base FluxFill configuration. This design ensures that LoRA's effect is isolated to the FluxFill first-frame generation stage, enabling controlled comparison.

The main experimental results reported in Chapter 4 (Run 006) were obtained using the base FluxFill model without LoRA adapters. A separate LoRA inference experiment (20 videos × 5 prompts = 100 edited videos) was conducted using the trained adapters at full strength (scale = 1.0), enabling a direct comparison between base and LoRA-adapted FluxFill on the same pipeline and evaluation framework. The results of this comparison are presented in Section 4.10.

3.5.4. Data Quality Considerations

The fully automated data generation pipeline introduces a specific quality risk: false positive training samples. The Qwen 2.5-VL-7B model must classify lane marking attributes (count, colour, pattern) from SAM 2-segmented road regions, and any misclassification propagates into the training data. For example, a region where lane markings are faded, partially occluded, or at a steep perspective angle might be labelled "single solid white" when the actual marking is double or dashed. The REQUIRE_CLEAR_ROAD filter mitigates this by rejecting samples where the VLM outputs "unknown" for any attribute, but it cannot catch misclassifications where the VLM provides a confident but incorrect label.

No manual audit of the training datasets was conducted within the scope of this thesis. The impact of potential false positives on LoRA training quality is examined in the experimental results (Chapter 4) and discussed in Chapter 5. This represents a known limitation of the automated data generation approach. While it enables dataset construction at scale (13,372 samples from 200 video chunks), it sacrifices the quality assurance that manual labelling provides.


3.6. Stage 3: Trajectory Analysis with Alpamayo

The third pipeline stage analyses whether the Alpamayo VLA model perceives and responds to the generated lane markings. This is done by running NVIDIA's Alpamayo-R1-10B VLA model (cf. NVIDIA et al., 2025) on both original and edited videos and comparing the predicted trajectories. The comparison uses the original ground- truth trajectory as a reference point, but it is important to note that because the generated lanes are intentionally different from the originals, the minADE delta does not measure generation quality. It measures the magnitude of the VLA model's behavioural response to the visual changes. CLIP Score and temporal consistency serve as the meaningful quantitative metrics for evaluating the generated video data itself.

3.6.1. The VideoPainter-to-Alpamayo Data Bridge

Alpamayo was designed to process clips from the PhysicalAI-AV dataset in a specific format, namely seven synchronised camera views, four consecutive frames per camera, ego-motion history, and ground-truth trajectory annotations. The VideoPainter output, a single edited MP4 video of one camera, is not directly compatible with this format. A custom data bridge was built to reconcile the two.

The bridge works by parsing the VideoPainter output filename, which encodes the clip UUID and camera name in the pattern:

    <clip_id>.<camera_name>_vp_edit_sample0.mp4

From the clip UUID, the bridge loads the corresponding full recording from the PhysicalAI-AV HuggingFace dataset, retrieving all seven camera views, ego-motion history (16 timesteps), and the ground-truth future trajectory (64 waypoints at 10 Hz, spanning a 6.4-second horizon).

Temporal alignment is handled through frame rate matching. The VideoPainter output is encoded at 8 fps, yielding a content time step of 0.125 seconds. The dataset loader is configured with this same time step so that the four frames it returns correspond to the same temporal window as the last four frames of the edited video. The video is decoded using PyAV, and the last four consecutive frames are extracted.

If the VideoPainter output dimensions differ from the dataset's native resolution, bilinear interpolation is used to resize the edited frames to match, ensuring pixel-level compatibility.

3.6.2. The Multi-Camera Black-Out Strategy

Alpamayo processes a seven-camera tensor as input, meaning 7 cameras times 4 frames equals 28 images. In a normal inference pass, all cameras provide meaningful visual information, and the model integrates cues from all views to predict the trajectory.

For evaluating the impact of inpainting, this multi-camera integration presents a confound. If the six unedited cameras are left unchanged, the model can use them to predict trajectories that effectively ignore the edited front camera, masking the effect of the lane marking modifications. To isolate the edited camera's influence, a deliberate design choice was made. All non-target cameras are set to black (all pixel values zeroed) during both the original-video and edited-video inference passes.

This means the model receives a tensor of the expected shape (7 cameras, 4 frames, 3 channels, height, width), but only camera index 6 (camera_front_tele_30fov) contains actual visual information. The remaining six cameras are uniformly black. The ego-motion history, 16 timesteps of past positions and rotations, remains identical between both passes and is always sourced from the real driving data.

This black-out strategy ensures that any difference in trajectory prediction between the original and edited passes can be attributed to the visual content of the front camera, rather than to information from other views. It is worth acknowledging that this is an artificial configuration. In a real deployment, all cameras would provide input, and the model's response to lane marking changes on one camera would be modulated by what it sees on the others. The black-out strategy therefore measures the maximum possible effect of the visual edit, that is, the upper bound on how much the lane marking change can influence trajectory prediction when the model has no alternative visual information to fall back on.

3.6.3. Dual-Pass Inference Protocol

For each edited video, two inference passes are performed:

Pass 1 (Original): Alpamayo receives the unmodified frames from the PhysicalAI-AV dataset on the front telephoto camera, with all other cameras blacked out. The model predicts a trajectory, and the minimum Average Displacement Error (minADE) against ground truth is computed. This establishes the baseline for this specific clip.

Pass 2 (Generated): The front telephoto camera's frames are replaced with the last four frames decoded from the VideoPainter output video. Everything else, the blacked-out non-target cameras, the ego-motion history, the ground-truth trajectory, remains identical. The model predicts a trajectory, and minADE is computed again.

The difference between the two minADE values, generated minus original, quantifies the impact of the lane marking edit on trajectory prediction for that specific video. A negative delta indicates that the edited video led to a more accurate trajectory (the model's prediction moved closer to ground truth), while a positive delta indicates degradation.

Inference parameters are fixed across all experiments:

    top_p:                    0.98
    temperature:              0.6
    max_generation_length:    256 tokens
    num_trajectory_samples:   1
    precision:                bfloat16 with flash-attention (cf. Dao et al., 2022)

The model is loaded once at the start of each workflow run and reused across all videos, avoiding the approximately 2-minute loading overhead for each individual video.

3.6.4. minADE Computation

The minimum Average Displacement Error is computed as described in Chapter 2 (Section 2.8.7):

    minADE = min over S samples of (1/T) * sum over t of
             || predicted_xy(t) - groundtruth_xy(t) ||_2

where S is the number of trajectory samples (set to 1 in the main experiments), T is the number of future waypoints (64, at 10 Hz over 6.4 seconds), and only the x and y coordinates (horizontal ground plane) are used. The z component is excluded. The metric is reported in metres.

3.6.5. Output Artefacts

Each video produces three output files:

An inference JSON file containing the predicted trajectory coordinates, minADE values for both original and generated passes, chain-of-thought reasoning traces from the VLA model, temporal configuration parameters, and compute metrics (inference time, GPU memory usage, RAM usage).

A visualisation NPZ file containing the full trajectory tensors (predicted, ground truth, and ego history) and all camera frames, enabling offline re-rendering of visualisations.

An overlay MP4 video showing the edited frames with projected 3D trajectories. The projection uses a pinhole camera model with the coordinate transform x_cam = -y_ego, y_cam = -z_ego, z_cam = x_ego, and focal length f_x = (W/2) / tan(FOV/2). Ground-truth trajectories are drawn in red, predictions in cyan, and a bird's-eye-view inset (250 x 250 pixels) in the bottom-left corner shows the trajectories from above. A progressive reveal mode linearly maps the frame index to the number of visible waypoints, creating an animation of the trajectory being drawn forward over time.

At the run level, an aggregate report summarises average, best, and worst minADE across all videos, along with total inference time and peak resource usage.

No upstream file was deleted.

The thesis contribution resides entirely in ten new files totalling approximately 3,900 lines. These files transform the upstream Alpamayo, which provides only a single-clip interactive inference notebook, into a production-grade batch evaluation system capable of processing hundreds of VideoPainter-edited videos and producing quantitative trajectory response metrics. What follows analyses each major component.

3.6.6.1. The VideoPainter-to-Alpamayo Data Bridge (run_inference.py)

At 640 lines, run_inference.py is the file that bridges the gap between VideoPainter's output format and Alpamayo's input expectations. Upstream Alpamayo loads clips directly from NVIDIA's PhysicalAI-AV dataset using a dedicated dataset class; it has no mechanism for processing externally edited videos, no batch processing, and no evaluation metrics.

The bridge operates through a filename parsing protocol. VideoPainter outputs are named with an embedded _edited_ token followed by the clip identifier and camera name (e.g., clip_123_edited_CAM_FRONT_LEFT.mp4). The run_inference.py file parses this naming convention to extract the clip_id and camera_name, then loads the original clip's full multi-camera frame set and ego-motion data from the PhysicalAI-AV dataset. Exactly one camera's frames are replaced with the VideoPainter-edited frames; all other cameras either retain their original frames or are blacked out according to the multi-camera strategy described in Section 3.6.2. The edited frames are bilinearly resized to Alpamayo's expected resolution of 576 x 1024 pixels.

The significance of this file lies in what it enables, namely the dual-pass inference protocol described in Section 3.6.3. For each video, the model runs inference twice, once with the original unedited frames and once with the VideoPainter-edited frames, under identical conditions, specifically the same temporal window, the same ego-motion history, and the same ground-truth trajectory. The only difference is the pixel content of a single camera. This A/B comparison is the methodological core of the evaluation. Any change in the predicted trajectory must be attributable to the visual changes introduced by VideoPainter, because all other inputs are held constant.

The file also computes minADE (minimum Average Displacement Error) against the ground-truth trajectory for both passes and produces structured JSON output containing predicted trajectory coordinates, minADE values, chain-of- thought reasoning traces from the VLA model, temporal configuration parameters, and compute metrics (inference time, GPU memory usage via nvidia-smi queries, and RAM usage via psutil). A companion NPZ file stores the full trajectory tensors, predicted, ground-truth, and ego-history, for offline visualisation.

The inference from this file's existence is that integrating a VLA model into a video editing evaluation pipeline is a non-trivial systems engineering problem. The upstream Alpamayo simply loads a dataset clip and runs forward inference. Converting that single-clip capability into a controlled A/B evaluation system, with filename-based data routing, selective frame replacement, resolution normalisation, dual-pass execution, and structured metric output, required building an entirely new inference frontend that understands both VideoPainter's output conventions and the PhysicalAI-AV dataset's multi-camera structure.

3.6.6.2. Trajectory Visualisation (visualize_video.py)

At 964 lines, visualize_video.py is the largest new file and provides the visual evidence that accompanies every quantitative result. It renders overlay videos showing predicted and ground-truth trajectories projected onto the camera frames, side-by-side comparison videos between original and edited passes, and bird's-eye-view trajectory insets.

The projection pipeline implements a pinhole camera model with a coordinate transform specific to the PhysicalAI-AV dataset's ego-centric coordinate system, where x_cam = -y_ego, y_cam = -z_ego, z_cam = x_ego, with focal length f_x = (W/2) / tan(FOV/2). Ground-truth trajectories are drawn in red, predictions in cyan. A bird's-eye-view inset (250 x 250 pixels) in the bottom-left corner shows both trajectories from above, providing spatial context that the perspective projection obscures. A progressive reveal mode linearly maps the frame index to the number of visible waypoints, creating an animation of the trajectory being drawn forward over time.

The file uses PyAV for H.264 encoding, writing directly to MP4 containers without intermediate frame files. It handles both single-camera overlay rendering and multi-camera comparison layouts, with camera-yaw-aware transforms that correctly orient the ego-to-camera projection for each of the dataset's camera positions (front, front-left, front-right).

The significance of this file is methodological. Quantitative metrics like minADE reduce complex trajectory behaviour to a single scalar; the visualisations provide the qualitative context needed to interpret that scalar. A minADE increase of 0.3 metres might reflect a harmless lateral shift or a dangerous trajectory deviation. Without the overlay videos, this distinction would be invisible. The visualisations thus serve as both an explanatory tool and a quality assurance mechanism. They enable visual inspection of every video where the metric indicates a large trajectory change.

3.6.6.3. Cloud Orchestration (workflow_alpamayo.py)

The workflow_alpamayo.py file (583 lines) defines the HLX cloud workflow that orchestrates Stage 3 of the pipeline. It manages GCS FuseBucket mounts for the Alpamayo-R1-10B checkpoint (accessed via FUSE filesystem rather than copied to each node), GCS data mounts for the VideoPainter output directory, and result upload to a structured GCS output prefix.

A critical design decision in this file is in-process model reuse. The Alpamayo-R1-10B model (10 billion parameters, requiring approximately 22 GB of VRAM in float16) is loaded once at the start of the workflow and reused for all videos in the batch. The upstream inference notebook loads the model per invocation. For a batch of 100 videos requiring 200 inference passes (dual-pass protocol), eliminating repeated model loading saves approximately 100 load cycles of 30-45 seconds each, a cumulative saving of close to an hour. The file implements this through a VLAVideoMetrics dataclass that encapsulates per-video results and a sequential processing loop that passes the pre-loaded model to each inference call.

The workflow file also implements aggregate reporting. After all videos are processed, it computes run-level statistics (average, best, and worst minADE across all videos for both original and generated passes) and writes them alongside timing information and peak resource usage to a summary JSON file. This aggregate report is the primary input to the quantitative analysis in Chapter 4.

3.6.6.4. Container Infrastructure

The Dockerfile (105 lines) builds on nvidia/cuda:12.1.1-cudnn8-devel-ubuntu22.04 with Python 3.12 and installs PyTorch 2.8, flash-attention (for efficient transformer attention in the 10B-parameter VLA model), and the HLX workflow SDK.

A notable detail is a monkey-patch applied to the physical_ai_av dataset library. The NVIDIA PhysicalAI-AV dataset package returns certain array fields via Apache Arrow's .to_numpy() method, which produces read-only arrays. The scipy.spatial.transform.Rotation module used by Alpamayo's geometry.py attempts to modify these arrays in place, causing a "read-only array" ValueError. The Dockerfile applies a sed command that wraps the .to_numpy() calls with np.array() to produce writable copies. This is a runtime bug in the upstream dataset library, not in Alpamayo itself, and represents the only third-party code fix in the entire Stage 3 pipeline.

The docker-compose.yaml (29 lines) provides local development configuration with GPU passthrough, 16 GB shared memory, and a Jupyter port mapping for interactive debugging. The .dockerignore (55 lines) excludes checkpoint files, NPZ outputs, and Python cache directories from the build context. The README_DOCKER.md (137 lines) documents the container build, configuration, and execution procedures. The scripts/build_and_run.sh (179 lines) automates Docker image building, tagging, pushing to Google Artifact Registry, and HLX workflow submission for Stage 3 of the pipeline.

3.6.6.5. Architectural Separation and Its Consequences

The decision to leave every line of the Alpamayo core library unmodified, zero changes across all 19 source files, including the VLA architecture, the diffusion trajectory head, and the geometry utilities, has the same three consequences identified for SAM 2 in Section 3.3.6.6.

First, reproducibility. The evaluation results reported in Chapter 4 are produced by the unmodified Alpamayo-R1-10B model. Any deviation in trajectory prediction between the original and generated passes is attributable to the visual content of the edited frames, not to modifications in the model code. This is essential for the scientific validity of the evaluation. The thesis claims that lane marking changes affect VLA trajectory prediction, and this claim would be compromised if the model itself had been altered.

Second, upgradeability. The application layer interacts with Alpamayo through its public inference API (model loading, forward pass, trajectory decoding). If NVIDIA releases a successor model (Alpamayo-R2, for instance) with the same API surface, the entire evaluation pipeline, filename parsing, dual-pass protocol, minADE computation, visualisation, would work without modification.

Third, attribution clarity. The trajectory prediction quality is entirely attributable to NVIDIA's model. The thesis contribution is the evaluation framework around that model, including the data bridge from VideoPainter's output format, the dual-pass comparison protocol, the quantitative metric computation, the trajectory visualisation system, and the cloud orchestration infrastructure.

3.6.6.6. Summary of the Technical Contribution

The approximately 3,900 lines of novel code transform Alpamayo from a single-clip research notebook into a production-grade batch evaluation system. The contribution is not a modification of the VLA model but the construction of everything needed to use that model as an evaluation instrument for video inpainting quality, including the VideoPainter-to-Alpamayo data bridge, the dual-pass comparison protocol, the minADE metric computation, the trajectory visualisation renderer, the cloud workflow orchestration, and the containerised deployment infrastructure.

The pattern is consistent across all three pipeline stages. SAM 2 required approximately 5,390 lines of application code around an unmodified core; VideoPainter required approximately 5,500 lines of new and modified code around a 98.8% identical core; Alpamayo requires approximately 3,900 lines around a 100% identical core. In each case, the foundation model provides the core capability, segmentation, inpainting, or trajectory prediction, and the thesis provides the domain-specific engineering layer that makes that capability applicable to the lane marking evaluation task at production scale. The total volume of novel code across all three stages (approximately 14,800 lines) quantifies the engineering effort required to bridge the gap between general-purpose foundation models and a specific, end-to-end research application.


3.7. Workflow Orchestration and Cloud Infrastructure

3.7.1. The Master Pipeline Orchestrator

All three stages are chained by a master workflow orchestrator (workflow_master.py, 712 lines) that defines a directed acyclic graph of three task nodes. Each task specifies its own container image, GPU allocation (one NVIDIA A100 80 GB), and cloud storage mounts. Data-dependency edges enforce strict sequential execution. Stage 2 cannot begin until Stage 1 completes, and Stage 3 cannot begin until Stage 2 completes.

The orchestrator itself runs in a lightweight container (Python 3.10-slim, approximately 500 MB) that contains no machine learning dependencies. Its sole purpose is to serialise the workflow graph and submit it to the execution platform. The heavy ML workloads are confined to the stage-specific containers.

A single run identifier (run_id) propagates through all three stages, providing end-to-end traceability. Stage 1 writes its output to a GCS path that includes the run_id; Stage 2 reads from that path and writes its own output to a different path with the same run_id; Stage 3 reads the Stage 2 output and writes final results. At any point, a user can reconstruct the complete provenance of a set of results by following the run_id through the GCS directory hierarchy.

3.7.2. Container Architecture

Four Docker containers are used:

The SAM 2 container is built on nvidia/cuda:12.1.1-cudnn8-runtime-ubuntu22.04 with Python 3.10. It includes SAM 2, Qwen2.5-VL (for caption generation during data generation workflows), FFmpeg, gcsfs for cloud storage access, and the workflow SDK. Image size is approximately 8 GB.

The VideoPainter container uses nvidia/cuda:12.1.1-cudnn8-devel-ubuntu22.04 with Python 3.10. It includes a modified Diffusers fork supporting CogVideoX, FluxFill, the Qwen2.5-VL-7B VLM, accelerate, PEFT, and the workflow SDK. Image size is approximately 15 GB.

The Alpamayo container uses nvidia/cuda:12.1.1-cudnn8-devel-ubuntu22.04 with Python 3.12, the only container requiring a different Python version. It includes PyTorch 2.8, flash-attention, the Alpamayo model source, a patched version of the physical_ai_av dataset library (to work around a read-only NumPy array issue in the ego-motion loader), and the workflow SDK. Image size is approximately 12 GB.

The master orchestrator container uses python:3.10-slim with only gcsfs and the workflow SDK installed. Image size is approximately 500 MB.

Each container is defined by a Dockerfile and a docker-compose.yaml in the corresponding stage directory, allowing developers to build and run any stage locally for debugging with a single docker compose build command.

3.7.3. Image Build, Tagging and Deployment

A single shell script (scripts/build_and_run.sh, 649 lines) automates the full cycle from source code to running workflow. When invoked, the script performs three operations in sequence for every stage included in the run.

First, it builds the Docker image locally using docker compose build from the stage's directory. Second, it tags the image twice, once with the master run identifier (e.g., harimt_sam2_06_p1_gen_video_20260222_174611) and once with a latest tag. Third, it pushes both tags to Google Artifact Registry at europe-west4-docker.pkg.dev, which serves as the central container registry accessible by the Helix workflow engine. This build-tag-push sequence ensures that every experiment runs against a uniquely identified image that can be traced back to the exact code state at launch time.

The script accepts a STAGES parameter (any combination of the digits 1, 2 and 3) that selects which stages to build and run. Running STAGES=123 builds all four images (including the master orchestrator) and submits the full pipeline; STAGES=3 builds only the Alpamayo container and submits a single-stage workflow that reuses existing VideoPainter outputs from a previous run. This selective-stage design was essential during development, where individual stages were iterated independently without rebuilding the entire pipeline.

After all images are pushed, the script serialises the workflow definition, container image URIs, GPU allocations, environment variables, GCS paths, prompt configurations, and submits it to the Helix execution platform. From the developer's perspective, a single command line invocation triggers the complete sequence of build, push, configure and launch.

3.7.4. Cloud Storage and Data Lineage

All data flows through Google Cloud Storage using a structured directory hierarchy:

    gs://<bucket>/<workspace>/
      input/data/camera_front_tele_30fov/chunk_NNNN/    (source videos)
      outputs/sam2/<run_id>/                             (segmentation)
      outputs/preprocessed_data_vp/<run_id>/             (VP-formatted data)
      outputs/vp/<run_id>/<instruction_folder>/          (edited videos)
      outputs/alpamayo/<run_id>/                         (trajectories)
      checkpoints/sam2/                                  (SAM 2 weights)
      checkpoints/videopainter/                          (CogVideoX, FluxFill)
      checkpoints/alpamayo/                              (Alpamayo-R1-10B)

Model checkpoints are mounted read-only via cloud storage FUSE (Filesystem in Userspace), which provides lazy-loading access without downloading the full checkpoint to local disk. Symlinks redirect container-expected file paths to the FUSE mount points. This avoids the latency and storage cost of copying multi-gigabyte model weights into each container at startup.

A custom URI scheme (chunks://) was developed for batch video selection from the chunk-structured dataset. The format:

    chunks://<bucket>/<prefix>?start=0&end=19&per_chunk=5

resolves to individual GCS paths by listing chunk directories and selecting the specified number of MP4 files from each. An early-stop optimisation terminates the listing as soon as enough files have been collected.


3.8. Experimental Design

3.8.1. Lane Marking Prompts

Five lane marking prompts are used, covering the primary attribute combinations of count, colour and pattern:

    p1: "Single solid white continuous line, aligned exactly to the original
         lane positions and perspective; keep road texture, lighting, and
         shadows unchanged"

    p2: "Double solid white continuous line, aligned exactly to the original
         lane positions and perspective; keep road texture, lighting, and
         shadows unchanged"

    p3: "Single solid yellow continuous line, aligned exactly to the original
         lane positions and perspective; keep road texture, lighting, and
         shadows unchanged"

    p4: "Double solid yellow continuous line, aligned exactly to the original
         lane positions and perspective; keep road texture, lighting, and
         shadows unchanged"

    p5: "Single dashed white intermitted line, aligned exactly to the original
         lane positions and perspective; keep road texture, lighting, and
         shadows unchanged"

Each prompt includes explicit instructions about alignment, perspective and context preservation. These instructions were developed iteratively through pilot experiments and were found to reduce artefacts compared to shorter, less specific prompts.

The five prompts were chosen to span the most common lane marking attributes encountered in international road standards. Single versus double lines, white versus yellow colours, and solid versus dashed patterns represent the dimensions along which lane markings vary most meaningfully. The set is not exhaustive, mixed-colour markings, triple lines, and other less common configurations are excluded, but it covers the configurations most likely to affect trajectory prediction.

3.8.2. Processing Variants

Each prompt is applied in two processing variants:

100frames: The original pipeline configuration, processing up to 100 frames per video (12.5 seconds at 8 fps).

vps90: VideoPainter strength set to 0.90. The strength parameter controls how aggressively the generation departs from the original pixels in the masked region, on a scale from 0 to 1. At 1.0, the output reflects none of the original pixels; at lower values, the generation retains more of the original content. At 0.90, the model generates with near-full strength while preserving a small influence from the source pixels. This variant was included to test whether slightly reduced generation strength yields comparable or improved visual quality.

3.8.3. Experiment Scale

The full experiment processes:

    100 source videos x 5 prompts x 2 variants = 1,000 edited videos

Each edited video undergoes dual-pass Alpamayo inference (original and generated), yielding 2,000 trajectory prediction passes. The total compute budget is approximately 200 A100 GPU-hours across all stages.


3.9. Strengths and Limitations of the Methodology

3.9.1. Strengths

Reproducibility. Every parameter, from the SAM 2 point grid coordinates to the LoRA training hyperparameters to the Alpamayo inference configuration, is fixed and documented. The containerised architecture means the entire pipeline can be rebuilt from Dockerfiles and run on any system with access to NVIDIA A100 GPUs and the required GCS paths. The shared run_id provides full data lineage from source video to final trajectory prediction.

Automation. No manual annotation, mask correction, or quality assessment is required at any stage. The bottom-half point grid eliminates the need for per-video segmentation prompts, the VLM refinement loop automates first-frame quality control, and the Alpamayo evaluation runs without human intervention. This makes the pipeline scalable to datasets much larger than the 100-video sample used here.

Behavioural responsiveness analysis. The use of trajectory prediction as a behavioural probe goes beyond what pixel-level and perceptual metrics can capture. It confirms that the generated lane markings are perceived by a downstream driving model, a dimension that CLIP Score and temporal consistency (the meaningful quality metrics) do not address directly.

Modularity. Each stage can be updated independently. If a better segmentation model becomes available, it can replace SAM 2 without touching the inpainting or evaluation stages, as long as it produces the same output format. This modularity also facilitated development, as each stage was built and tested in isolation before integration.

3.9.2. Limitations

Full-road masks. The segmentation stage produces masks covering the entire visible road surface. Lane-specific masks, which would allow editing individual lanes independently, are not implemented. This means the inpainting model must generate lane markings for the entire road in a single pass, which is a harder task than editing one lane at a time and may explain some of the semantic errors observed in the generated content.

Single-camera evaluation. The black-out strategy, while effective at isolating the edited camera's influence, creates an artificial input configuration that no production ADAS system would encounter. The measured trajectory impacts represent upper-bound estimates; in a real multi-camera system, the effect of lane marking changes on one camera would be diluted by information from the others.

Base FluxFill versus LoRA-adapted FluxFill. The main experimental results (Run 006, 1,000 videos) use the base FluxFill model without domain-specific LoRA adapters. A separate LoRA inference experiment (100 videos) was conducted using the trained adapters at full strength. However, the LoRA experiment used only 20 videos (versus 100 in the base run), limiting statistical power for per-prompt comparisons. The LoRA results are presented in Section 4.10.

Automated training data quality. The LoRA training data was generated entirely by an automated pipeline using Qwen 2.5-VL-7B for lane attribute captioning. No manual quality audit of the resulting datasets was performed. As discussed in Section 3.5.4, the VLM may produce false positive labels, confident but incorrect lane attribute classifications, that contaminate the training data. This is a fundamental limitation of the fully automated approach and is identified as the most probable cause of the negative LoRA results observed in Chapter 4.

Ego-motion as confound. The ego-motion history (past 16 positions and rotations) is identical for both inference passes. The Alpamayo model can partially infer the trajectory from deceleration or acceleration patterns in this history, independent of visual input. This means some of the trajectory prediction is not influenced by the visual edit at all, which may attenuate the measured effect of lane marking changes.

Dataset scope. The 100-video sample from a single camera view provides meaningful statistical observations, but it may not generalise to all driving scenarios, camera configurations, or geographic regions. The PhysicalAI-AV dataset reflects specific recording conditions, and the pipeline's performance on fundamentally different driving environments (e.g., unpaved roads, construction zones, heavy snow) has not been tested.

Temporal consistency reliance. The temporal coherence of generated videos depends entirely on CogVideoX's inherent architecture. No explicit optimisation for lane marking geometry, such as enforcing that line positions remain fixed across frames or that dashed patterns repeat at consistent intervals, is applied. This reliance on the general-purpose model's temporal capabilities may explain why more geometrically complex prompts (double lines, dashed patterns) show lower CLIP scores and higher caption refinement failure rates.
