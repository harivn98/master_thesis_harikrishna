2. LITERATURE REVIEW AND THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

The research conducted in this thesis sits at the intersection of several fields that have, until recently, developed largely in isolation: video segmentation, image and video inpainting, diffusion-based generative modelling, vision-language understanding, and vision-language-action models for autonomous driving. This chapter traces how each of these fields evolved, identifies the key publications and methodological turning points, and explains how the theoretical concepts behind them inform the design decisions taken in this work. Where relevant, the discussion also addresses alternative approaches that were considered and experimentally evaluated during the development of the pipeline, providing the rationale for the final model selections.

The literature search underlying this chapter was conducted across IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, SpringerLink, Google Scholar and arXiv, using search terms including "video inpainting," "diffusion inpainting," "road segmentation autonomous driving," "vision-language-action model," "lane marking detection," and "parameter-efficient fine-tuning." Backtracing through the bibliographies of key papers, particularly (Bian et al., 2025), (Ravi et al., 2024) and (NVIDIA et al., 2025), provided additional sources that shaped the review.


2.1. Image and Video Segmentation

Segmentation, assigning a meaningful label to every pixel or region in an image, is one of the oldest problems in computer vision, and its solutions have gone through at least three distinct eras. Understanding this progression matters here because the first stage of the pipeline developed in this thesis relies on a segmentation model to identify road surfaces in driving videos, and the choice of that model was informed by practical experimentation with several alternatives.

2.1.1. Convolutional Approaches

Deep learning entered the segmentation landscape with the fully convolutional network (FCN) (Long et al., 2015). The insight was straightforward but powerful. Classification architectures such as VGGNet could be turned into pixel-wise prediction networks by replacing the final fully connected layers with convolutional ones. The resulting model could accept inputs of any spatial resolution and produce a correspondingly sized label map, a property that previous approaches lacked.

Around the same time, U-Net was introduced (Ronneberger et al., 2015), an encoder-decoder architecture with skip connections that became something of a template for dense prediction tasks. The contracting encoder captured increasingly abstract features while the expanding decoder recovered spatial detail, and the skip connections ensured that fine-grained information was not lost during downsampling. Although U-Net was designed for biomedical images, its architecture later proved foundational in an entirely different context, as it became the standard backbone for denoising networks inside diffusion models, a connection that will become important in Section 2.4.

Deeper networks became feasible with the residual learning framework (He et al., 2016), where identity shortcut connections allowed training of networks exceeding 100 layers. ResNet backbones were quickly adopted by the segmentation community, most notably in the DeepLab family. DeepLabv3+ (Chen et al., 2018) combined atrous (dilated) convolutions with an encoder-decoder design to achieve 89.0% mIoU on PASCAL VOC 2012. The atrous spatial pyramid pooling module, which applies parallel dilated convolutions at multiple rates to capture context at different scales, became a widely imitated design pattern.

DeepLabv3+ was one of the segmentation models evaluated during the early stages of this thesis for extracting road regions from autonomous driving footage. While it produced reasonable segmentation on individual frames, it required fine-tuning on road-specific datasets to achieve acceptable accuracy, and, more critically, it operated on a per-frame basis with no built-in mechanism for temporal consistency across video sequences. When processing hundreds of videos, occasional frame-level failures would propagate unpredictably into the downstream inpainting pipeline.

Instance segmentation, the task of distinguishing individual objects within the same class, was tackled with Mask R-CNN (He et al., 2017), which extended the Faster R-CNN detection framework with a parallel mask prediction branch. This unified detection and segmentation within a single architecture, though its reliance on bounding-box proposals made it less suited for amorphous regions like road surfaces.

2.1.2. Transformers Enter the Picture

The Transformer architecture, originally developed for machine translation (Vaswani et al., 2017), found its way into computer vision through the Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021). ViT split images into fixed-size patches, embedded each patch as a token, and processed the resulting sequence through a standard Transformer encoder. The key advantage over convolutional networks was the self-attention mechanism's ability to capture relationships between distant image regions without the locality constraint inherent in convolution.

One practical obstacle was computational cost, since global self-attention scales quadratically with the number of tokens. The Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2021) addressed this through a hierarchical design that computed attention within local windows and introduced cross-window connections via a shifted windowing scheme. Swin achieved 87.3% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet and 53.5 mIoU on ADE20K for segmentation, surpassing convolutional baselines by a considerable margin.

These hierarchical vision Transformers later informed the design of segmentation foundation models, which bring us to the models most directly relevant to this thesis.

2.1.3. Segmentation Foundation Models and the Move to Video

The paradigm of universal, promptable segmentation was introduced by the Segment Anything Model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023). Trained on over one billion masks spanning 11 million images, SAM could segment essentially any object given a prompt, whether a point, a bounding box, or a text description. The architecture had three pieces: a ViT-based image encoder, a flexible prompt encoder, and a lightweight mask decoder. SAM demonstrated impressive zero-shot transfer across diverse segmentation tasks, establishing the idea that a single model could replace task-specific segmentation pipelines.

SAM (the original, image-only version) was tested in the early experimental phase of this thesis for road segmentation. It performed well on individual frames, but it had no mechanism for propagating masks across a video sequence. Each frame was processed independently, which meant that a single frame where the road was poorly segmented, due to shadows, occlusion, or unusual road geometry, could produce a mask that was inconsistent with its neighbours. For a video inpainting pipeline that expects smooth, temporally coherent masks, this inconsistency was problematic.

The successor, SAM 2 (Ravi et al., 2024) extended the promptable segmentation paradigm to video. The critical addition was a streaming memory architecture comprising three components: a memory encoder that compressed per-frame features and predictions into compact representations, a memory attention module that allowed each frame to attend to stored memories from earlier frames, and a memory bank that maintained a fixed window of recent and prompted memories. Trained on the SA-V dataset (approximately 35,500 videos with 642,600 masklets), SAM 2 achieved real-time inference at over 44 frames per second while maintaining temporal mask consistency across entire video sequences.

It is worth noting that an alternative approach was also explored before settling on SAM 2, namely using Grounding DINO, an open-set object detection model, to first detect the road region via a text prompt (e.g., "road" or "road surface") and then feeding the resulting bounding box to SAM for mask refinement. In practice, this two-stage detection-then-segmentation strategy proved unreliable at scale. Grounding DINO's road detections were inconsistent across frames. In some frames the road was correctly identified, while in others the model failed to detect it altogether or returned bounding boxes covering buildings or sky. Since SAM 2's video predictor propagates masks from an initial prompt across all subsequent frames, a single failed detection on the seed frame would cause the entire video's segmentation to fail. The detection bottleneck made this approach unsuitable for processing hundreds of videos without manual intervention.

The approach ultimately adopted in this thesis uses SAM 2's video predictor with a fixed set of seed points placed exclusively in the bottom half of the frame, with a reference point at the bottom centre. The geometric prior behind this strategy is straightforward. In any front-facing vehicle camera, the road surface always occupies the lower portion of the image, and the bottom centre of the frame always lies on the road directly ahead of the ego vehicle. By anchoring the seed prompt at this location and selecting the connected component that contains it, the pipeline avoids the need for an external detection model entirely. SAM 2's memory mechanism then propagates this initial mask through the entire video with temporal consistency, which is exactly what the downstream inpainting stage requires. The full technical details of this strategy, including the four-stage morphological post-processing pipeline, are described in Chapter 3.


2.2. Image Inpainting

Image inpainting, filling in missing or damaged regions of an image with plausible content, has undergone several paradigm shifts over the past decade. This section traces the trajectory from classical methods through GANs to the diffusion-based models that underpin the pipeline developed in this thesis.

2.2.1. Patch-Based and GAN-Based Methods

The earliest computational inpainting methods worked by copying patches from known regions of the image into the missing area, using structural and textural similarity as guidance. These exemplar-based approaches were effective when the missing region was small and the surrounding texture was repetitive, but they could not hallucinate content that did not already exist elsewhere in the image, a fundamental limitation for tasks like lane marking generation, where the desired content may be entirely absent from the original scene.

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) offered a way around this limitation. By training a generator to produce outputs that a discriminator could not distinguish from real data, GANs enabled the synthesis of novel content rather than mere copying. GAN-based inpainting methods such as DeepFillv2 (Yu et al., 2019) introduced gated convolutions that learned to selectively attend to valid pixels, enabling support for free-form (irregular) masks. The visual results could be impressive, but GANs brought their own difficulties, including mode collapse (where the generator converges on a narrow range of outputs), training instability, and persistent struggles with large or semantically complex missing regions. For the lane marking generation problem considered here, where the mask covers the entire road surface and the desired content must adhere to specific textual specifications, GAN-based methods lacked the controllability needed.

2.2.2. Diffusion Models for Image Inpainting

The arrival of diffusion models changed the landscape considerably. The core idea, described in detail in Section 2.4, is to train a neural network to reverse a gradual noising process. The key insight for inpainting is that the denoising can be conditioned on the known (unmasked) regions of an image along with a binary mask, so the model generates only the missing content while respecting the surrounding context.

Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) (Rombach et al., 2022) made this computationally tractable by performing the diffusion process in the compressed latent space of a pretrained autoencoder rather than directly on pixels. The latent representation is typically four to eight times smaller in each spatial dimension, reducing computational requirements by roughly an order of magnitude. Cross-attention layers enabled text conditioning, meaning a user could specify what content should appear in the masked region via natural language. LDMs became the foundation of the Stable Diffusion family and set the template that nearly every subsequent text-conditioned diffusion model has followed.

The most recent step in this progression is the FLUX architecture from Black Forest Labs, described in "Scaling Rectified Flow Transformers for High-Resolution Image Synthesis" (Esser et al., 2024). FLUX replaces the U-Net denoising backbone used by Stable Diffusion with a rectified flow Transformer that learns straight-line trajectories between noise and data distributions. Its inpainting variant, FluxFill, accepts a binary mask and a text prompt and generates high-resolution, text-guided content in the masked region. FluxFill represents the current state of the art for single-image inpainting and is used in this thesis as the first-frame generator within the dual-model video inpainting pipeline described in Chapter 3.


2.3. Video Inpainting

Extending inpainting from single images to video introduces a fundamental additional requirement. The filled content must be not only spatially plausible within each frame but also temporally consistent, free of flickering, juddering, or abrupt changes, across the entire sequence. Achieving this temporal coherence while simultaneously allowing semantic control over the generated content has been the central challenge in video inpainting research.

2.3.1. Flow-Based and Propagation Methods

Early deep learning approaches to video inpainting leaned heavily on optical flow. E2FGVI (Li et al., 2022) was one of the first end-to-end trainable frameworks that combined flow completion, feature propagation and content hallucination in a single model. The idea was to estimate how pixels move between frames (optical flow), use those motion fields to warp content from neighbouring frames into the missing regions, and then refine the result with a hallucination module for areas where no source content was available. Integrating all three steps in a single network avoided the error accumulation that plagued earlier multi-stage pipelines, where mistakes in flow estimation directly degraded the final output.

ProPainter (Zhou et al., 2023) pushed this further with dual-domain propagation, combining image-based and feature-based propagation to handle both short-range and long-range temporal dependencies, along with a mask-guided sparse Transformer that attended only to regions indicated by the mask. The result was a +1.46 dB PSNR improvement over prior methods on standard benchmarks. The Spatial-Temporal Transformer Network (STTN) (Zeng et al., 2020) had earlier shown that Transformer attention could effectively model temporal relationships for inpainting, though it too remained a reconstruction method.

A shared limitation across all flow-based video inpainting methods is that they are designed for reconstruction. They fill missing regions with content that is plausible given the surrounding visual context, but they offer no mechanism for specifying what that content should be through text or other semantic signals. For the lane marking generation problem addressed in this thesis, this distinction is crucial. The goal is not to reconstruct what was there before, but to generate new content, lane markings with specific attributes, that was never present in the original video. This requires text-guided semantic editing capability, which only became available with diffusion-based approaches.

2.3.2. Diffusion-Based Video Inpainting and the Selection of VideoPainter

The extension of diffusion models to video generation brought the possibility of text-guided video inpainting. CogVideoX (Yang et al., 2024) introduced a video diffusion architecture built around what the authors called an Expert Transformer, a variant that uses expert adaptive layernorm for conditioning and 3D full attention that jointly processes text and video tokens. The model operates in a compressed latent space through a 3D causal VAE with 4x temporal and 8x8 spatial compression, generating videos at 480x720 resolution with up to 49 frames per clip.

Building on CogVideoX, VideoPainter (Bian et al., 2025), currently the state of the art for text-guided video inpainting. The key architectural contribution is a dual-stream design that separates background preservation from foreground generation. One stream, a lightweight copy of the video diffusion backbone acting as a context encoder, processes the masked video (the background regions that should be preserved) and produces context features. The other stream, the main CogVideoX backbone, receives these context features through addition at each Transformer block and generates the foreground content in the masked regions, guided by a text prompt. VideoPainter also supports any-length video inpainting by dividing long sequences into overlapping clips, with the overlapping frames providing temporal continuity between segments. On the DAVIS benchmark, the method achieved a PSNR of 33.75, SSIM of 0.982 and LPIPS of 0.016 for object removal, and a CLIP Score of 28.79 for text-guided editing.

The selection of VideoPainter as the video inpainting model in this thesis was the outcome of an experimental evaluation that included several alternatives. Three other models were tested: LTX-Video 2 (an open-source video generation model), Stable Diffusion 1.5 XL (adapted for video through temporal attention layers), and Hunyuan Video (Tencent's video generation framework). Each was evaluated on a small set of autonomous driving videos with road surface masks and lane marking text prompts. LTX-Video 2 struggled with maintaining temporal consistency across more than a few frames, as the generated lane markings tended to shift position or change appearance between frames. Stable Diffusion 1.5 XL, while producing reasonable individual frames, lacked native video understanding and required frame-by-frame generation with post-hoc temporal smoothing, which introduced visible artefacts at frame boundaries. Hunyuan Video produced temporally smoother results but its text-guided editing capabilities were limited; it was primarily designed for text-to-video generation rather than inpainting, and adapting it for mask-conditioned editing required modifications that compromised output quality. VideoPainter, by contrast, was specifically designed for mask-conditioned, text-guided video editing with native temporal consistency, which was precisely the combination of capabilities required for the lane marking generation use case. Its dual-stream architecture preserved the unchanged portions of the road while generating semantically controlled content in the masked region, and its overlapping-clip processing handled videos of arbitrary length.

2.4. Diffusion Models: Theory and Architecture

Diffusion models provide the generative foundation for both the image inpainting (FluxFill) and video inpainting (CogVideoX / VideoPainter) components of this thesis. This section covers their mathematical formulation, the architectural shifts from U-Nets to Transformers, and the conditioning mechanisms that make text-guided generation possible.

2.4.1. Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models

The modern formulation of diffusion-based generation was presented in the Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) paper (Ho et al., 2020). The framework defines two processes. A forward process progressively adds Gaussian noise to a data sample x_0 over T timesteps:

    q(x_t | x_{t-1}) = N(x_t; sqrt(1 - beta_t) * x_{t-1}, beta_t * I)

where beta_1 through beta_T is a predefined variance schedule. An important property is that x_t can be sampled directly from x_0 without iterating through intermediate steps:

    q(x_t | x_0) = N(x_t; sqrt(alpha_bar_t) * x_0, (1 - alpha_bar_t) * I)

with alpha_bar_t = product_{s=1}^{t} (1 - beta_s). For large enough T, x_T is essentially standard Gaussian noise.

The reverse process is where the generation happens. A neural network (originally a U-Net) learns to predict the noise at each step, and the data sample is recovered iteratively:

    p_theta(x_{t-1} | x_t) = N(x_{t-1}; mu_theta(x_t, t), sigma_t^2 * I)

Training reduces to a surprisingly simple objective: a mean squared error between the actual noise added and the network's prediction of it:

    L_simple = E_{t, x_0, epsilon} [ || epsilon - epsilon_theta(x_t, t) ||^2 ]

This straightforward loss was shown to be sufficient to produce high-quality samples, achieving an FID of 3.17 on CIFAR-10, competitive with GANs but without the training instability.

2.4.2. Faster Sampling with DDIM

A practical drawback of DDPMs is the need for many denoising steps, typically a thousand, to produce good outputs. This was addressed with Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIMs) (Song et al., 2021), which construct a family of non-Markovian diffusion processes sharing the same training objective as DDPMs but admitting much faster reverse processes. DDIMs can skip intermediate timesteps via deterministic update rules, enabling 10x to 50x faster sampling without any retraining. The deterministic nature of DDIM also enables meaningful interpolation in latent space, a property later exploited for image editing and inversion tasks.

2.4.3. Latent Diffusion Models

Running diffusion in pixel space remains expensive, especially for high-resolution images. A pivotal architectural change was introduced (Rombach et al., 2022), performing the entire diffusion process in the compressed latent space of a pretrained autoencoder. The Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) architecture consists of three pieces:

First, an autoencoder (encoder E, decoder D) trained to compress images into a latent representation z = E(x). The encoder typically reduces each spatial dimension by a factor of four to eight, and the decoder reconstructs images from these compressed representations.

Second, a denoising network (originally a U-Net) that operates entirely in this latent space, predicting the noise component epsilon_theta(z_t, t, c) where c represents optional conditioning information.

Third, conditioning mechanisms, most importantly cross-attention layers that inject text embeddings into the denoising network. The cross-attention is computed as:

    Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(Q * K^T / sqrt(d)) * V

where Q = W_Q * phi(z_t) are queries derived from the latent features, K = W_K * tau(c) and V = W_V * tau(c) are keys and values projected from the text encoding tau(c). This mechanism allows the text prompt to guide what content is generated at each spatial location.

Operating in latent space cuts computational cost by roughly an order of magnitude compared to pixel-level diffusion, with comparable or better generation quality. The LDM architecture became the basis for Stable Diffusion and established the template that essentially all subsequent text-to-image and text-to-video diffusion models have followed.

2.4.4. Classifier-Free Guidance

One of the most practically important techniques for steering conditional diffusion models is classifier-free guidance (Ho et al., 2022). During training, the conditioning signal is randomly dropped with some probability, so the model learns to generate both conditionally and unconditionally. At inference, the final noise prediction is a weighted blend of the two:

    epsilon_hat = epsilon_theta(z_t, t, null) + w * (epsilon_theta(z_t, t, c) - epsilon_theta(z_t, t, null))

where w > 1 is the guidance scale. Increasing w pushes generated samples closer to the conditioning signal, essentially trading output diversity for stronger adherence to the prompt. This technique has become ubiquitous; virtually every modern text-conditioned diffusion system, from Stable Diffusion to FLUX to CogVideoX, relies on it.

2.4.5. From U-Nets to Transformer Backbones

The original diffusion models used U-Net as their denoising backbone, but this choice was more historical convention than theoretical necessity. The Diffusion Transformer (DiT) (Peebles et al., 2023) demonstrated that a standard Transformer operating on latent patches could replace the U-Net entirely, and that generation quality scaled predictably with model size, a property that U-Nets had not convincingly exhibited. The largest DiT-XL/2 variant achieved a state-of-the-art FID of 2.27 on class-conditional ImageNet at 256x256 resolution.

Both CogVideoX and FLUX adopted Transformer-based backbones. CogVideoX uses an Expert Transformer with 3D full attention across spatial and temporal dimensions, while FLUX employs a rectified flow Transformer that models straight-line trajectories between noise and data distributions. The rectified flow formulation (Esser et al., 2024), which learns a velocity field that follows straight paths from noise to data, achieves better sample quality than the DDPM training objective while needing fewer sampling steps. This transition from U-Nets to Transformers represents the current frontier in diffusion model architecture and underpins both generative models used in this thesis.

2.4.6. Extending Diffusion to Video

Generating video rather than still images requires modelling temporal dynamics on top of spatial structure. CogVideoX (Yang et al., 2024) tackles this through three architectural elements. First, a 3D causal VAE encodes video sequences with 4x temporal compression and 8x8 spatial compression; the causal design ensures each frame's encoding depends only on itself and preceding frames, supporting autoregressive generation. Second, the Expert Transformer uses separate parameter sets (expert adaptive layernorm) for text and video tokens within the same attention block, enabling joint processing via 3D full attention while maintaining modality-specific normalisation. Third, the model is trained progressively across increasing resolutions and frame counts, starting from short, low-resolution clips and scaling to 480x720 resolution with 49 frames.

CogVideoX serves as the video generation backbone for VideoPainter and, by extension, for the temporal propagation stage of the pipeline developed in this thesis.


2.5. Vision-Language Models

Vision-language models (VLMs) connect visual perception with language understanding. Two kinds of VLMs are relevant to this work, contrastive models that learn aligned image-text representations, and generative VLMs that produce natural language descriptions of visual scenes.

2.5.1. CLIP: Contrastive Vision-Language Alignment

CLIP, or Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training (Radford et al., 2021), is a model trained on 400 million image-text pairs using a contrastive objective. An image encoder (ViT or ResNet) and a text encoder (Transformer) are trained jointly so that matching image-text pairs have high cosine similarity in a shared embedding space, while non-matching pairs are pushed apart.

This shared embedding space turns out to be useful in several ways that are directly relevant to this thesis. As a zero-shot classifier, CLIP can categorise images into arbitrary text-described categories. As a quality metric, CLIP Score, the cosine similarity between an image embedding and a text embedding provides a measure of how well a generated image matches its prompt. And as a conditioning mechanism, CLIP text embeddings serve as the signal that cross-attention layers in diffusion models use to guide generation. CLIP Score is one of the standard evaluation metrics used in Chapter 4 to assess whether generated lane markings align with their textual descriptions.

2.5.2. Generative VLMs and Their Role in This Thesis

Generative VLMs go beyond aligned embeddings by producing free-form natural language outputs conditioned on visual inputs. The paradigm of instruction-tuned multimodal models was established by LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023), which connected a CLIP visual encoder with the Vicuna language model through a projection layer and trained the combination on GPT-4-generated instruction- following data. Despite its architectural simplicity, LLaVA demonstrated that this approach could support sophisticated visual conversations.

More capable models followed. The Qwen2.5-VL Technical Report (Bai et al., 2025) describes a VLM that supports native dynamic resolution (handling images of any aspect ratio without resizing), video understanding at one frame per second, and a range of grounding tasks including detection, pointing and counting. Qwen2.5-VL achieves state-of-the-art performance across numerous visual understanding benchmarks.

In this thesis, Qwen2.5-VL serves two distinct functions. During the training data generation phase (Chapter 3), it generates structured captions that describe the lane marking attributes visible in each video frame, extracting count (single or double), colour (white or yellow), and pattern (solid or dashed) into a normalised textual format. During the video editing phase, it serves as an automated quality assessor within an iterative refinement loop. After FluxFill generates a candidate first frame, Qwen2.5-VL evaluates whether the result matches the intended lane marking specifications and, if not, produces a revised caption with more specific visual details. This feedback cycle continues until the VLM judges the output acceptable or a maximum iteration count is reached. The use of a VLM as an in-loop quality critic for generative inpainting appears to be novel, as no prior work in the reviewed literature employs this pattern.

It is worth noting that VideoPainter's original codebase uses OpenAI's GPT-4 via a proprietary API for its visual evaluation functions. In this thesis, all such API calls were replaced with the locally running Qwen2.5-VL-7B, making the entire pipeline self-contained, with no external API keys, no per-call costs, and no transmission of automotive data to third-party servers. This substitution is not merely an engineering convenience; it is a precondition for reproducibility and for deployment in industrial environments where data governance constraints prohibit sending imagery to external services.


2.6. Vision-Language-Action Models for Autonomous Driving

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models represent a recent paradigm shift in autonomous driving research. Rather than decomposing the driving task into separate perception, prediction and planning modules, VLA models attempt to unify all three within a single architecture that takes visual input, reasons about the scene in natural language, and outputs numeric trajectory predictions.

2.6.1. The Shift from Modular Pipelines

Traditional autonomous driving stacks consist of a cascade in which perception modules detect objects and lane markings, prediction modules forecast other agents' future trajectories, a planner generates the ego vehicle's path, and a controller translates that path into steering and throttle commands. Each module is trained and optimised separately, and information is inevitably lost at each handoff between components.

This modular approach was challenged with UniAD (Hu et al., 2023), a unified framework that connected perception, prediction and planning through a shared query-based representation, enabling joint optimisation across the entire chain. The improvements in planning performance demonstrated that tighter integration across modules was beneficial, setting the stage for even more unified approaches.

2.6.2. VLA Models and Alpamayo

The VLA paradigm takes unification further by incorporating a large language model as the central reasoning backbone. The model processes multi-view camera images, textual context and historical ego-motion through a single architecture and produces both language-based reasoning traces and numeric trajectory coordinates.

Alpamayo-R1-10B (NVIDIA et al., 2025) is a 10-billion parameter VLA model with three capabilities. Its visual perception component encodes multi-view camera images through a visual encoder and projects them into the language model's token space. Its chain-of-thought reasoning capability generates natural language descriptions of the perceived scene, identifying traffic participants, assessing hazards, articulating the planned driving strategy, forcing the model to "think before acting," which improves performance on unusual (long-tail) driving scenarios. And its trajectory prediction head outputs future ego-vehicle waypoints as numeric (x, y) coordinates at regular time intervals. The primary evaluation metric is minimum Average Displacement Error (minADE):

    minADE = min_k  (1/T) * sum_{t=1}^{T} || hat{s}_t^k - s_t ||_2

where hat{s}_t^k is the k-th predicted waypoint at time t, s_t is the ground truth, T is the prediction horizon, and the minimum is taken over K prediction modes.

Alpamayo was trained in two stages, first supervised fine-tuning on a large driving dataset with chain-of-thought annotations, then reinforcement learning with trajectory-accuracy-based rewards. The RL stage yielded significant improvements, particularly on long-tail scenarios.

In this thesis, Alpamayo is employed in a role it was not originally designed for, serving as a behavioural responsiveness probe for video inpainting. The idea is that conventional metrics like PSNR and SSIM measure pixel-level differences but say nothing about whether an edit would actually be perceived by an autonomous driving model. By running Alpamayo twice on each video, once with the original frames and once with the inpainted frames, and comparing the resulting minADE values, it becomes possible to confirm whether the VLA model perceives and responds to the lane marking modifications. However, because the generated lanes are intentionally different from the originals, the minADE delta does not measure generation quality but rather the magnitude of the VLA model's behavioural response. CLIP Score and temporal consistency are the meaningful quantitative metrics for evaluating the generated video data.

To isolate the effect of the edited video, a deliberate experimental design choice was made. Only the front telephoto camera (camera_front_tele_30fov) is fed the VideoPainter-edited frames, and all other six camera views are set to black (zeroed-out tensors). Alpamayo natively operates on a seven-camera multi-view input, and without this blacking-out strategy, the model could rely on unedited views from adjacent cameras to predict trajectories, potentially masking the effect of the lane marking edits on the front camera. By providing visual information exclusively through the edited camera, the model must base its trajectory prediction on the generated content. The ego-motion history (past 16 positions and rotations) is kept identical between both inference passes, so differences in trajectory prediction can be attributed to the visual changes rather than motion cues. This dual-pass, single-camera evaluation protocol appears to be novel in the literature, as no prior work has used a VLA model to assess the functional impact of video inpainting on driving behaviour.


2.7. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning

Large pretrained models can be adapted to domain-specific tasks through fine-tuning, but updating all parameters of a multi-billion parameter model is computationally prohibitive in most practical settings. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods offer a way around this constraint.

The most widely adopted PEFT technique is LoRA, or Low-Rank Adaptation (Hu et al., 2022). The core observation is that the weight updates learned during fine-tuning tend to occupy a low-dimensional subspace. For a pretrained weight matrix W_0 in R^{d x k}, LoRA decomposes the update as:

    W = W_0 + Delta_W = W_0 + B * A

where B in R^{d x r} and A in R^{r x k}, with rank r much smaller than both d and k. Only the low-rank matrices A and B are trained; the original weights stay frozen. For typical configurations, d = k = 4096, r = 16, this reduces the number of trainable parameters by over 99%. This approach was shown to match or exceed full fine-tuning performance on GPT-3 175B, at a fraction of the memory and compute. A notable practical advantage is that at inference time, the low-rank matrices can be merged back into the original weights (W = W_0 + B * A), introducing zero additional latency.

In this thesis, LoRA is applied to adapt FluxFill for domain-specific lane marking generation. By training low-rank adapters on curated datasets of road images paired with structured lane marking captions, FluxFill can be specialised for generating particular lane marking patterns, single white solid, double yellow dashed, and so on, without the cost of retraining the full model. The training data for these LoRA adapters is itself generated automatically through the SAM 2 + Qwen2.5-VL data generation pipeline described in Chapter 3.


2.8. Evaluation Metrics

Evaluating generative models, and video inpainting in particular, requires metrics that capture different dimensions of quality. This section reviews the metrics used in the experimental evaluation of Chapter 4.

2.8.1. Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR)

PSNR expresses the ratio between the maximum possible signal and the reconstruction error, measured in decibels:

    PSNR = 10 * log10( MAX^2 / MSE )

where MAX is the maximum pixel value (255 for 8-bit images) and MSE is the mean squared error between original and reconstructed images. Higher values indicate closer pixel-level agreement. PSNR is a standard baseline metric, but it correlates poorly with human perceptual judgements and is not particularly informative for semantic editing tasks where the goal is to change the content rather than preserve it.

2.8.2. Structural Similarity Index (SSIM)

SSIM was proposed (Wang et al., 2004) as a metric that better captures human perception by evaluating three components, luminance, contrast and structural similarity, which are combined multiplicatively:

    SSIM(x, y) = [l(x, y)]^alpha * [c(x, y)]^beta * [s(x, y)]^gamma

Values range from -1 to 1, with 1 indicating perfect structural match. SSIM outperforms PSNR in correlating with perceived quality, particularly for structural distortions, though it still operates at the pixel level.

2.8.3. Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS)

LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018) moves beyond pixel comparisons by computing distances between deep feature representations extracted from a pretrained network (typically AlexNet or VGGNet). The idea is that perceptual similarity should be measured in a learned feature space rather than in pixel space. Lower LPIPS values indicate greater perceptual similarity. LPIPS has been shown to correlate more strongly with human quality judgements than both PSNR and SSIM.

2.8.4. CLIP Score

CLIP Score measures semantic alignment between generated content and a text description by computing the cosine similarity of their CLIP embeddings:

    CLIP_Score = cos(E_image(x), E_text(c))

In the context of video inpainting for lane marking generation, CLIP Score evaluates whether the generated road content matches the textual prompt that specified the desired lane attributes. It captures semantic intent in a way that pixel-level metrics cannot.

2.8.5. Temporal Consistency

Temporal consistency quantifies the visual coherence between consecutive frames, computed as the average structural similarity across adjacent frame pairs:

    TC = (1 / (N-1)) * sum_{i=1}^{N-1} SSIM(f_i, f_{i+1})

Values near 1.0 indicate smooth, flicker-free video. This metric is particularly important for video inpainting, where the diffusion model generates content independently for overlapping clips that must blend seamlessly.

2.8.6. Frechet Inception Distance (FID)

FID (Heusel et al., 2017) measures the distributional distance between real and generated images in the feature space of an Inception v3 network:

    FID = || mu_r - mu_g ||^2 + Tr(Sigma_r + Sigma_g - 2 * (Sigma_r * Sigma_g)^{1/2})

Lower FID indicates that the generated distribution more closely matches the real distribution. FID remains the most commonly reported metric for assessing overall generation quality across generative modelling research.

2.8.7. Minimum Average Displacement Error (minADE)

minADE is the standard trajectory prediction metric in autonomous driving. Given K predicted trajectory modes, it selects the mode closest to the ground truth and reports the average displacement:

    minADE = min_k (1/T) * sum_{t=1}^{T} || hat{s}_t^k - s_t ||_2

Lower values indicate more accurate trajectory prediction. In this thesis, minADE takes on a novel role: rather than evaluating a trajectory prediction model's accuracy, it serves as a measure of how much video inpainting affects downstream driving behaviour. The delta between minADE on original and edited videos, computed per-video and aggregated across experiments, provides a functionally grounded assessment of inpainting impact that transcends what any pixel-level metric can offer.


2.9. Theoretical Framework

The literature reviewed in the preceding sections converges into a theoretical framework built on four pillars, through which the experimental findings of Chapters 4 and 5 are interpreted.

The first pillar is foundation model composition. The pipeline chains three independently developed foundation models, SAM 2 for segmentation, CogVideoX and VideoPainter for inpainting, and Alpamayo for evaluation, into a single system. None of these models were designed to work together; each exposes a well-defined interface (binary masks, text-conditioned video, trajectory predictions) that permits inter-model communication without joint training. The viability of this approach rests on the zero-shot generalisation capabilities of each model, that is, their ability to perform adequately on autonomous driving data despite having been trained largely on general-purpose datasets. The selection of specific models (SAM 2 over SAM and DeepLabv3+; VideoPainter over LTX-2, Stable Diffusion XL and Hunyuan Video) was driven by empirical evaluation, as documented in Sections 2.1.3 and 2.3.2.

The second pillar is text-guided generative editing. The diffusion framework (Section 2.4) provides the mathematical machinery for conditioned generation. The conditional noise prediction epsilon_theta(z_t, t, c) allows textual specifications of lane marking attributes to steer the content generated in masked regions. Classifier-free guidance (Section 2.4.4) controls the trade-off between fidelity to the prompt and output diversity. The dual-model architecture explored in this thesis, FluxFill for first-frame semantic precision, CogVideoX for temporal coherence, represents a hypothesis that combining models specialised at different temporal scales produces better results than either model in isolation. This hypothesis is tested experimentally in Chapter 4.

The third pillar is automated quality assessment through vision-language models. Qwen2.5-VL's ability to perform fine-grained visual understanding (Section 2.5.2) is leveraged in the iterative caption refinement loop, where the VLM acts as a critic evaluating whether generated lane markings satisfy the intended specification. The theoretical basis is the empirically demonstrated capability of large VLMs to reason semantically about visual content, a capability that, while practically effective, lacks formal guarantees of correctness or completeness.

The fourth pillar is behavioural responsiveness analysis through trajectory prediction. Conventional inpainting metrics (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS) assess pixel- level quality, while CLIP Score and temporal consistency measure semantic alignment and temporal coherence respectively, and these are the meaningful metrics for generation quality. By using Alpamayo to compare trajectories predicted from original versus inpainted videos, with all non-target cameras blacked out to isolate the effect, this thesis introduces a behavioural probe that confirms whether the VLA model perceives the generated lane markings. The minADE delta (Section 2.8.7) quantifies the magnitude of the VLA model's behavioural response to the visual changes, though because the generated lanes are intentionally different from the originals, the delta does not measure generation quality. The chain-of-thought reasoning traces provide qualitative evidence of how visual changes influence driving decisions. This evaluation approach assumes that a well-calibrated VLA model's response to visual modifications serves as a meaningful proxy for how a production ADAS system would react, an assumption that is reasonable given Alpamayo's state-of-the-art trajectory accuracy, though it remains an approximation rather than a guarantee.

Together, these four pillars define the lens through which the experimental results are analysed. Technical performance is assessed through generative quality metrics (Pillar 2), semantic correctness through VLM feedback (Pillar 3), and functional impact through trajectory prediction (Pillar 4), all enabled by the modular composition of foundation models (Pillar 1).
