1. INTRODUCTION

Lane markings are, in a sense, the simplest element of road infrastructure, painted lines on asphalt, and yet they carry an outsized importance for autonomous driving. They tell a vehicle where it may travel, where it must not, and how to position itself within a lane. Every lane keeping assist system, every highway autopilot, every trajectory planner depends on correctly perceiving these markings. When the perception fails, because a marking is faded, unusual, or simply of a type the model has not seen enough of, the consequences can range from uncomfortable lane drift to dangerous lateral positioning.

The trouble is that training a model to recognise all the lane markings it might encounter requires data that contains all of them. Widely used autonomous driving datasets, including nuScenes (Caesar et al., 2020) and the Waymo Open Dataset (Sun et al., 2020), provide valuable multi-camera footage, but they reflect the roads where they were recorded. The same limitation applies to NVIDIA's PhysicalAI-Autonomous-Vehicles collection, the dataset used in this thesis, which offers high-quality multi-camera driving footage, yet its lane marking distribution is inevitably shaped by the regions in which the recordings were made. A fleet operating primarily on German motorways will amass plenty of white dashed and solid lines. Double yellow markings, common under North American road standards, will be scarce. Faded single lines on rural roads may barely appear at all. This distributional imbalance is not a shortcoming of any particular dataset; it is a structural feature of the data collection process. And it directly undermines the robustness of models trained on that data.

One response is to collect more. Another is to simulate. Graphics engines such as CARLA (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017) can render synthetic driving scenes with configurable lane markings, but the visual gap between rendered and real footage remains noticeable, and models trained partly on synthetic data often struggle when transferred to real-world inputs (cf. Tobin et al., 2017). A third response, and the one pursued in this thesis, is to edit existing real footage by taking a genuine driving video, identify the road surface, and replace the lane markings with something different, guided by a text description of what should appear. If done well, the result is a video that retains the lighting, perspective, road texture and temporal dynamics of a real recording but contains lane markings that were never actually there.

Diffusion-based generative models (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022) have made this plausible. Recent video inpainting systems such as VideoPainter (Bian et al., 2025) can fill masked regions of a video with text-specified content while maintaining frame-to-frame coherence, which is exactly the capability needed for controlled lane marking generation. But a question that has received far less attention is whether these edits actually matter to the systems they are meant to help. Conventional evaluation metrics such as PSNR, SSIM (Wang et al., 2004), LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018), and CLIP Score (Radford et al., 2021) measure how an edited video looks relative to the original or relative to its text prompt, but they say nothing about how a downstream driving model would respond to the changes. A lane marking that scores well on visual metrics might be entirely ignored by a trajectory planner, or it might cause the planner to predict a significantly different path. Without closing that loop, the practical value of generative data augmentation for autonomous driving remains uncertain.

This thesis addresses both sides of the problem. It presents the design, implementation and experimental evaluation of an end-to-end pipeline that generates controlled lane marking variations in driving videos and then measures their functional impact on trajectory prediction. The pipeline chains three independently developed foundation models, a video segmentation model, a video inpainting system, and a Vision-Language-Action trajectory prediction model, into a single orchestrated workflow. The technical details of each model, along with the alternatives that were evaluated and the reasons they were not selected, are covered in the literature review (Chapter 2) and the methodology (Chapter 3). The present chapter focuses on why this work matters, what gaps it addresses, and what it sets out to achieve.


1.1. Subject Relevance

Lane markings exist at the intersection of civil infrastructure and machine perception. International standards, including the Vienna Convention on Road Signs and Signals (United Nations, 1968), codify their use for delineating lanes, indicating permitted manoeuvres and communicating regulatory information. For ADAS systems, lane markings are typically the primary input to lateral control. Lane detection algorithms identify the markings, trajectory planners use them to compute a desired path, and the vehicle's steering responds accordingly. When modern lane detectors are tested on challenging benchmarks such as CULane (Pan et al., 2018), they can achieve F1 scores above 81 percent (cf. Honda et al., 2023), but their accuracy drops markedly when faced with marking types that were underrepresented in training.

The scale of the data problem is considerable. A robust lane detection or trajectory prediction model needs exposure to single lines, double lines, white markings, yellow markings, solid patterns, dashed patterns, mixed configurations, and degraded conditions, all across varying lighting, weather and road geometries. Acquiring this coverage through physical data collection alone would require recording fleets operating across multiple continents and road standards, which is logistically and financially impractical for most development teams.

Generative video editing offers a different path. Rather than recording new data, it modifies existing footage to produce variations that are otherwise unavailable. The appeal is straightforward. Start with a real video that has the right lighting, road texture and driving dynamics, and change only the lane markings to match a desired specification. If the generative model is good enough, the result is a photorealistic video with controlled lane attributes that can be added to the training set. If, additionally, the effect of this edit on downstream models can be measured, then it becomes possible to assess not just whether the generated data looks right, but whether it is useful.

The convergence of several recent advances makes this approach feasible for the first time. Video segmentation foundation models such as SAM 2 (Ravi et al., 2024) can now automatically identify road surfaces in driving footage without task-specific training. Video diffusion models (cf. Ho et al., 2022) can fill masked regions with text-guided content while maintaining temporal coherence. Vision-language models such as Qwen 2.5-VL (Bai et al., 2025) can evaluate whether generated content matches a textual specification. And Vision-Language-Action models such as Alpamayo-R1 (NVIDIA et al., 2025) can predict driving trajectories from video, providing a functional measure of how visual changes affect driving behaviour. The literature underpinning each of these capabilities is reviewed in Chapter 2.


1.2. Own Interest and Motivation

This thesis grew out of a prior internship at Mercedes-Benz AG (April to October 2025), during which the author worked within the autonomous driving data infrastructure of the company and encountered the practical constraints of lane marking diversity in real-world datasets first-hand. Certain marking configurations appeared in abundance while others, particularly those reflecting road standards outside the primary recording region, were difficult to find in the data at all.

The rapid development of generative models during 2024 and 2025 suggested a different approach. Rather than relying on additional physical data collection or on traditional augmentation techniques (colour jittering, geometric distortion), it appeared possible to edit existing driving footage semantically by specifying through natural language what the lane markings should look like and having a generative model produce the result. The idea of connecting such a generative system to a trajectory prediction model, thereby measuring whether the edits change how a driving model behaves, provided a research direction that combined academic novelty with direct industrial relevance.


1.3. Research Gap

Several gaps in the current research landscape motivate this work:

First, video inpainting methods have been developed and benchmarked as general-purpose tools. No prior work has adapted them specifically for the controlled generation of lane marking attributes, count, colour, pattern and spatial alignment, in autonomous driving footage. The semantic precision required for this task goes beyond what standard inpainting benchmarks measure.

Second, video diffusion models are optimised for temporal consistency but not for per-frame semantic detail. The first frame of an inpainted video sets the visual reference that the temporal model propagates, making its quality disproportionately important. No prior work has explored combining a specialised image inpainting model for first-frame generation with a video diffusion model for temporal propagation, that is, a dual-model architecture that separates the two concerns.

Third, text prompts for inpainting are typically written once and used as-is. There is no established mechanism for iteratively refining prompts using automated visual feedback, despite the well-documented sensitivity of diffusion models to prompt wording. Where visual quality assessment exists in inpainting pipelines, it typically relies on proprietary API calls to closed-source models (e.g., GPT-4), creating external dependencies that compromise reproducibility and prevent deployment in data-sensitive industrial environments.

Fourth, evaluation of video inpainting in autonomous driving contexts has relied exclusively on pixel-level and perceptual metrics. No prior work has used a trajectory prediction model to assess whether inpainted content changes how a driving system would actually behave, which is, for ADAS applications, the metric that matters most.

Fifth, the engineering challenge of chaining a segmentation model, an inpainting model, and a trajectory evaluation model into a reproducible end-to-end pipeline has not been addressed. These models operate in isolated environments with incompatible dependencies, and no orchestration framework exists to connect them with shared data lineage.


1.4. Objectives and Goals

The primary objective is to design, implement and evaluate an end-to-end pipeline for text-guided semantic video inpainting that generates controlled lane marking variations in driving videos and quantitatively assesses their impact on trajectory prediction.

The specific goals are:

Goal 1: Build an automated road segmentation pipeline that produces accurate binary masks from dashcam videos without manual annotation, suitable as input to downstream video inpainting.

Goal 2: Implement a dual-model video inpainting architecture where an image inpainting model generates a semantically precise first frame and a video diffusion model propagates the edit temporally, with lane marking attributes controlled through natural language prompts.

Goal 3: Develop an automated quality assurance mechanism using a vision-language model that evaluates each generated frame against the editing instruction and iteratively refines the prompt until the output is semantically correct.

Goal 4: Create a data generation and filtering pipeline that automatically produces curated training datasets for domain-specific fine-tuning of the image inpainting model on lane marking attributes.

Goal 5: Integrate a Vision-Language-Action trajectory prediction model as a behavioural responsiveness probe, confirming that the generated lane markings are perceived by a downstream driving model through comparison of trajectory predictions between original and edited videos.

Goal 6: Orchestrate all pipeline stages into a unified, containerised, cloud-native workflow with end-to-end data lineage and traceability.

Goal 7: Conduct large-scale experiments across multiple lane marking prompts and processing variants to produce quantitative evidence on the relationship between text-guided video inpainting and autonomous driving behaviour.


1.5. Research Questions

RQ1: Can an automated video segmentation pipeline produce road surface masks of sufficient quality for downstream video inpainting, without requiring manual annotation or interactive prompts?

RQ2: Does a dual-model inpainting architecture, with separate models for first-frame generation and temporal propagation, produce semantically more accurate lane markings than a single video diffusion model alone?

RQ3: Can a vision-language model serve as an effective automated quality assessor within an iterative prompt refinement loop, and does this mechanism improve the semantic correctness of the generated output?

RQ4: How do different lane marking attributes, count, colour and pattern, affect inpainting quality as measured by text-video alignment, temporal consistency and VLM-based assessment?

RQ5: What is the quantitative impact of text-guided lane marking modifications on trajectory prediction, and does this impact vary across lane marking types?

RQ6: Is it feasible to orchestrate a three-stage pipeline across heterogeneous containerised environments into a reproducible, cloud-native workflow suitable for industrial ADAS development?


1.6. Scope and Limitations

Scope:

The pipeline processes front-facing camera videos from the NVIDIA PhysicalAI-AV dataset, specifically the front telephoto camera view (30-degree field of view). Lane marking editing covers five representative prompts spanning the primary attribute combinations of single and double count, white and yellow colour, and solid and dashed patterns. Experiments are conducted on 100 source videos, each processed with all five prompts in two processing variants (default VideoPainter strength and 0.90 VideoPainter strength), producing 1,000 edited videos in total. The strength parameter controls how aggressively the generation departs from the original pixels in the masked region, where a value of 1.0 means the output reflects none of the original pixels and lower values retain more of the original content. All compute is performed on NVIDIA A100 80GB GPUs within the Mercedes-Benz Helix cloud environment. The trajectory evaluation model, Alpamayo-R1 (NVIDIA et al., 2025), is used in its publicly released form, without reinforcement learning post-training or route conditioning, as these components were not available at the time of writing.

Limitations:

The pipeline currently produces full-road masks, meaning the entire road surface is marked for inpainting. Lane-specific masks that would allow editing individual lanes independently are not implemented. Fine-tuning of the image inpainting model was conducted for the five lane marking prompts used in this thesis; extending the fine-tuning to additional lane marking attribute combinations remains as future work. The trajectory prediction model was used as released and was not fine-tuned for this task; its predictions reflect the pre-trained model's behaviour and may not fully represent a production ADAS system. Temporal consistency relies on the video diffusion model's inherent design and is not explicitly optimised for lane marking geometry. The evaluation dataset of 100 videos from a single camera view provides meaningful statistical observations but may not generalise to all driving scenarios, camera configurations or geographic regions. All experiments were conducted offline; integration with real-time ADAS processing pipelines is beyond the scope of this work.


1.7. Structure and Overview

The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows.

Chapter 2 reviews the literature across the four research domains that converge in this work, video segmentation, image and video inpainting, diffusion-based generative models, and vision-language-action models, and establishes the theoretical framework through which the experimental results are interpreted. It also documents the alternative models that were evaluated during the development of the pipeline and the rationale for the final selections.

Chapter 3 describes the methodology, covering the technical implementation of each pipeline stage, the experimental design, and the cloud-native workflow orchestration.

Chapter 4 presents the experimental findings across all three stages, including segmentation performance, inpainting quality metrics, caption refinement outcomes, generation timing, and trajectory prediction results.

Chapter 5 discusses the findings in the context of the research questions, analyses the relationship between lane marking attributes and both inpainting quality and trajectory impact, and addresses the limitations of the study.

Chapter 6 summarises the contributions, discusses their implications for autonomous driving development, and identifies directions for future work.
