FOREWORD

Some theses begin with a tidy research question that was clear from the start. This one did not. It grew out of a practical frustration that became harder to ignore the longer I spent working with autonomous driving data.

During an internship at Mercedes-Benz AG in Sindelfingen (April to October 2025), within the Maps and Perception team of the RD/ASP organisation, I spent several months working with HD map data. The datasets were enormous, carefully collected and meticulously managed, and yet, when it came to lane markings, they were inevitably skewed. German motorways produce certain kinds of lines in abundance, white, solid, predictable. But ask the data for double yellow dashed markings, or for the sort of faded single-line configurations common on rural roads in other countries, and the cupboard was often bare. It was not a flaw in how the data was gathered; it was simply an unavoidable consequence of recording in one part of the world and expecting the results to generalise everywhere else.

At the same time, generative models were advancing at a pace that made it difficult to keep up. By late 2024, diffusion-based systems could produce video content that was, at first glance, indistinguishable from footage captured by a real camera. The question that lodged itself in my thinking was not whether these models could make convincing video, clearly they could, but whether the things they generated would actually make a difference to the systems that needed to use the data. If you edit a lane marking in a driving video, does the car's prediction model notice? Does it change its planned trajectory? Or is the edit cosmetically impressive but functionally irrelevant?

That question became the centre of this thesis. Answering it required building something that did not exist, a pipeline connecting a segmentation model, an inpainting system and a trajectory prediction model into a single chain, where the output of each stage feeds the next. None of these components were designed to work together. They ran in different Python versions, demanded different GPU memory profiles, and expected their data in incompatible formats. A considerable part of the work, more than I initially anticipated, turned out to be engineering, writing Dockerfiles, debugging container networking, managing intermediate artefacts on cloud storage, and designing a workflow orchestrator that could sequence the stages reliably without manual intervention.

The Mercedes-Benz Helix cloud environment made this feasible. Having access to GPU-equipped compute nodes, shared storage buckets and a workflow scheduler meant that experiments could run at a scale that would have been impractical on a single workstation. It also imposed a useful discipline. Every component had to be containerised, every data handoff had to be explicit, and every run had to be traceable. Writing research code under those constraints felt more like building infrastructure than prototyping models, but the resulting system is one that someone else could, in principle, pick up and run without needing to replicate my local environment.

On a personal level, this project has been a lesson in thinking about systems rather than individual algorithms. The interesting problems were rarely about a single model's performance in isolation; they were about how models interact, how data degrades as it passes through transformations, and how to design an experiment that actually isolates the variable you care about. Deliberately editing lane markings in real driving footage and then measuring whether a trajectory model responds to those edits raises questions that go beyond technical metrics; questions about what it means to train safety-critical systems on synthetic data, and what responsibilities come with the ability to alter the scenarios those systems learn from.

None of this would have been possible without the support of supervisors, colleagues and mentors on both the university and industry sides. Their willingness to engage with a project that sat awkwardly between computer vision research and software engineering, and that changed direction more than once along the way, made the difference between a set of disconnected experiments and the integrated system described in the chapters that follow.

This foreword is not a preview of the results. It is an attempt to explain where the work came from and what it felt like to do it. The technical content begins in Chapter 1.
