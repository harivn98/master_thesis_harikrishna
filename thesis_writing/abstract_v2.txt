ABSTRACT

Advanced Driver Assistance Systems require diverse training data covering all lane marking configurations, yet real-world collection remains expensive and geographically constrained. This thesis investigates whether generative video inpainting can synthesize realistic lane marking variations and whether such edits meaningfully influence downstream trajectory prediction.

A three-stage, cloud-orchestrated pipeline was developed. In the first stage, SAM 2 (Ravi et al., 2024) segments the road surface from dashcam video using a bottom-half six-point seed grid, followed by four-stage morphological post-processing that yields hole-free binary masks without manual annotation. In the second stage, a dual-model inpainting architecture combines FluxFill (Black Forest Labs, 2024) for semantically precise first-frame generation with the CogVideoX-5B backbone from VideoPainter (Bian et al., 2025) for temporally consistent video propagation. An iterative caption-refinement loop driven by a locally deployed Qwen 2.5-VL model (Bai et al., 2025), replacing the original OpenAI GPT-4 dependency, verifies text-image alignment before video synthesis. In the third stage, NVIDIA Alpamayo-R1-10B (NVIDIA et al., 2025), a Vision-Language-Action model, runs trajectory predictions on both original and edited frames in a dual-pass protocol that confirms whether the generated lane markings are perceived by a downstream driving model.

Experiments on 100 source videos across five prompts and two processing variants, 1,000 edited videos total, demonstrate temporal consistency exceeding 0.985 and CLIP-based text–video alignment scores of 19.2–23.2, establishing these as the meaningful quality metrics for the generated data. Trajectory analysis shows per-video shifts of roughly one metre, confirming that the VLA model perceives and responds to the synthetic lane markings, though assessing whether the resulting trajectories are appropriate for the generated lane configurations requires visual inspection.

These findings establish, for the first time, a closed-loop framework connecting generative video editing to autonomous driving behaviour, demonstrating not only that synthetic lane markings achieve high visual quality, but that a downstream driving model treats them as meaningful visual input.
